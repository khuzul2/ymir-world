# X·C · THE SILVER ISLES & THE COUNCIL OF PEOPLES
## *the Curator's own ground: Elendor, the Little Folk at home, and the Crystal Seat as it actually sits; with extracts from the accounts of this book's making*

**Set down at the Crystal Seat of Elendor by the Magister-Compiler of the Crystal Seat — called in this book, and content to be called, the Curator; with extracts from the Compendium's book of accounts, kept by Master Fennick, Gnome, bursar to the Council of Peoples.**

> *Curator's note.* For every other chapter it has been my office to introduce the narrator, weigh
> his bias, and retire to the notes. For this one I could find no narrator to introduce. I asked a
> Tylweth Teg chronicler, who answered that the Isles were not going anywhere and neither was she.
> I asked a Gnome, once — which is the number of times one asks a Gnome. There remains the
> compiler: who was born here, and who once wrote of a better scholar that a man describing his
> mother's house counts the windows and not the cracks. That sentence has waited eleven chapters to
> collect. I bind this leaf at the reader's mid-ocean, where the sailor anchors; the impropriety of
> a compiler compiling himself I cannot avoid, every road by which I might commission this account
> ending at my own desk. — *the Curator*

---

I have spent this book at the edge of other people's pages, and the middle of a page turns out to
be a windier place than the notes ever showed me. Very well: the reader has had the world from a
priest, an archmage, a skald, a singer, a cat, and a drowned sailor; let him have one small country
from a clerk — and watch the clerk work against the grain, for I have promised cracks, and I am a
native, and natives count windows.

**Of the Isles.** East away from the sailor's home waters, mid-ocean, green and silver (Chapter V;
I cannot better him), lies the archipelago the charts call the **Silver Isles**. Of the name I have
heard four derivations: the birch-woods of the interior; the mica sand of the eastern bays; a
worn-down Fae word the Fae decline either to confirm or to supply; and the plain fact that at dusk
the sea here decides to be silver, whatever the weather argued all day. I trust the fourth, which
is not scholarship, and say so.

Know the older fact under the pretty one: **the Isles are old land and new islands.** Before the
year 0 they were no isles at all, but the high shoulders of the continent the Catastrophe drowned —
the rim of the country whose heart is now the Maelstrom's water. When the working broke the world,
the sea came in between the high ground and the body of it; we are, to be exact, the coastline of a
country that no longer exists. On the western shores at low spring tide a paved road walks down
into the water and keeps going, in good repair, toward a horizon with nothing on it. The oldest
songs of the Tylweth Teg name the year 0 in their own fashion — the Day the Road Drowned — and do
not sing it for visitors. I print the name with permission, which took nine years.

**Of Elendor.** The harbour the sailor called the prettiest on the ring is my earliest memory, and
I decline to be exact about it, for reasons that will close this chapter. Facts, then. Elendor is
built at two scales and polite about both: the quay-doors carry a lesser door within the leaf of
the greater, and a visitor learns inside an hour which knocker is his. Every people's flag flies
somewhere in the road; the town's industry is other peoples' business, in both senses; its vice is
dice — Ramiro lost four doubloons and a bosun's hat to us, and as a native I can settle the
dispute: the dice are not loaded. Nothing in Elendor is anything so simple as loaded. Lantern Row
shows forty-one windows to the quay. I know because I counted them as a boy, from the water,
waiting out my father's shift at the boom-chain. I notice that I have just counted them again, and
not one crack. I begin again.

**Of the Tylweth Teg.** The registers abroad call the Isles the kingdom of the **Little Folk**,
smallest of the pure peoples; the Little Folk call the Isles home, and keep the kingdom in a
cupboard for treaties. (There is a crown. It is small. It travels in a hatbox. I have carried the
hatbox.) And hold to Mistress Quondam's warning: the Little Folk are **two peoples** — the **Fae**,
who call themselves **Tylweth Teg**, and the **Gnomes**, who are not their menfolk but another race
entire.

The Tylweth Teg stand under three feet, come in both sexes whatever the ballads insist, and live
somewhat past a hundred and forty years — long enough to remember your grandfather's promise, and
they do. Elendor is their port and parlour; their true towns lie deep in the birch and oak of the
interior, and the visiting learned call them toy-perfect, an injustice only a neighbour notices: a
toy is made to please the hand that holds it, and nothing in a Fae town is made to please anyone
but the resident. The proportions are not quaint; they are *correct*, and it is the rest of the
world that is built approximately. Tall guests lodge at the wood's edge, under the guest-eaves,
welcome in a way that has a boundary in it. Dwarves the Fae do not distrust; dwarves they cannot
*parse*: it is minuted that at the Naughrith trade-embassy of 3419 the Fae delegate asked why a
people would dig toward the dark on purpose, and the dwarves, having conferred, asked why a people
would live where the wind could find them — both questions stand open on the order-paper still.

And the hatred — I must write it, for a native who omits it is counting windows. The Tylweth Teg
hate what the Maelstrom has touched: past argument, past commerce, past their mildness in every
other matter. A Fae harbour-warden will turn back a hull for one lightless pearl in the manifest
and stand in front of the anchor-chain until it goes; Maelstrom-flotsam on the west shores is
burned the same tide, by wardens who volunteer. No statute stands behind this; none has been
needed. It has been called, in this very hall, a hatred out of all proportion to their size — and I
confess that even I, their neighbour, have written it down as temperament. It is the page of this
chapter I am least sure of.

> *"Out of all proportion," writes the neighbour, and for once the exact man is inexact. The Fae
> line was seeded from **Seelie**, and the seeding left it what the makers' stock-books would call
> a calibration: alone among the warm peoples, the fairy-kind perceive the **Corrupted Essence**
> directly — as stench, as toothache, as a wrong note held and held. The hatred is not temperament;
> it is an instrument, reading. The warden at the anchor-chain is the only soul on the quay who can
> smell what the lightless pearl grew around. The free peoples set their one shared table in the
> smallest folk's harbour and called it courtesy. Read it properly: nothing Maelstrom-touched has
> ever sat a session in that hall unnoticed. The Council did not choose Elendor. Elendor is why
> there is still a Council.*

**Of the Gnomes.** They hold the high stony middles of the isles, one holding to a valley, walled
orchards, doors that do not face each other. Their wish is to be left alone, and it includes other
Gnomes: they gather for burials, boundary-walks, and audits, and linger longest at the audits. A
Gnome's hospitality is a door that opens once; I have had my once, and it was the finest evening of
my professional life, and I will not describe it, description being a way of going back. What the
world should know is what the Council learned in 2595: a people that wants nothing from you audits
you beautifully. The Seat's books have been kept by Gnomes since the first sitting; Master Fennick
has kept them forty years, has spoken with me perhaps eleven times, and each conversation has saved
the Council money.

**Of the Crystal Seat.** The history briefly, the Annalist having told it at scale. About **1700
d.M.** the **First Council of Peoples** convened, to hear the evidence that Ea's kind had outlived
Ea; in **1735** the peoples swore the **Coalition of Peoples** — one oath, against the Sovereign
Demons, in two clauses the lawyers have never bettered: the *sword-clause*, that the Enemy of one
is the Enemy of all; and the *lamp-clause*, that what any people learns of the Enemy is owed to
every people, at once and unpriced. Two hundred years of peace followed, the longest the fallen
world has kept. From **2590 to 2595** the Second Council renewed the oath and raised the **Crystal
Seat** above my harbour, and the **Council of Peoples** has sat here since — the nearest thing Ymir
has to a united government, a famous sentence I set down exactly because both halves bear weight:
*nearest thing*; and *has*. I noted in the Ages chapter that it was Xebechani diplomacy that
summoned the First Council, star-priests going embassy by embassy, and that the Church's
chroniclers have declined the debt ever since. Let the host's chapter pay it: in eighteen hundred
years, the Xebechani chair in this hall has never once stood empty. It is the only attendance they
have ever cared to keep perfect, and I have never learned why it should be this one.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *He pays the debt and will not price it. Elsewhere he scolded the friar's passive voice — "the
> peoples were summoned" — and restored the summoners; here, on his own page, he wonders at the
> eldest people's perfect attendance and lets the wonder lie. So ask it for him: why did the
> star-priests, who want nothing the warm world sells, spend a century of embassies assembling a

> oiled. The other peoples attend the Council. The Xebechani* maintain *it.*

Of the hall itself the sailor said "a building like weather persuaded to stop," and I have hung his
map beside this book inside it. The crystal of its walls was quarried in no pit any mason will
name; it takes the evening light and holds it the way deep water holds pressure, and in the great
chamber a whisper walks the whole ring of chairs, which has shortened more speeches than any rule
of order. I have read the minutes of the building years; I have read everything. Four pages are
missing from 2592, the year the foundations were cut. I record the fact. The reader may file it
with the derivations of "Silver."

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The four pages were dig-notes. The founders' masons cut for foundations and stopped early, on a
> floor that was already there: dressed, seamless, level past any mason's art, and warm in winter.
> The famous crystal was never brought to the hill; it was grown from it — upward, out of what that
> floor roofs, the way a pearl is grown around a grievance. The Isles are the standing rim of the
> drowned country; the Seat crowns the rim's high place; and the old empire built where it meant to
> return. I do not say what is under the Council of Peoples. The pages that said were removed by
> someone with better access than the Curator, which is a short list, and worth your while.
> "Weather persuaded to stop." Ask what the weather was asked to stop over.*

**And how it sits.** One chair to a people was the founders' rule, and the rule lasted until Men —
who are one people the way weather is one weather — required nine. The full Council, the **Great
Sitting**, convenes each tenth year, or at the summons of any three chairs; between sittings the
resident envoys meet as the **Table of Residents**, which does the work and knows it. Nothing is
carried by count of heads: a motion is read at three sittings and carries when no chair speaks
against it the third time — the *three askings* — so the Council moves at the speed of its most
patient objector. The reader will laugh; the reader is invited to find, in his own realm's history,
a treaty that has held eighteen hundred years, and to laugh again more carefully. Still, I promised
cracks. The **Long Question** — whether the half-peoples should have a chair — was moved in 2601
and resolved in 2642, by carpentry: a bench, which seated everybody and satisfied nobody, the
Council's characteristic success. The Qi-Long's six kingdoms share one chair by rotation and have
twice spent a whole sitting on whose decade it was. The Gaerwath keeps its chair as a duellist
keeps a glove, and sends, when it sends anyone, proxies chosen with care to insult. The Sohleugir
attend once in a generation, decline the chair — chairs, they observe, are for drying — deliver
their soundings in exact weights, and are gone by the tide. And the Belligerent Kingdoms have asked
for a chair once in three centuries, through an envoy who spat first; the asking is still on the
order-paper, which at our pace signifies interest.

**Of what the oath still binds.** The reader deep in the Equilibrium's calm may ask what an
alliance against the Sovereign Demons is *for*, most of the Sovereigns being dead. I answer as the
Seat answers new envoys: three walk; a continent stands on the southern charts that stood on no
chart before the Tide; and the lamp-clause has never lapsed — the reports of the risen coast are
read at every Great Sitting, in a chamber that goes notably quiet for them. The sword-clause has
been paid in full twice: once in the swearing generation, and once in the Chaos, when the
Coalition, so long a parchment, became again a sword — the Annalist's phrase, and for once he and I
would not amend each other. No signatory has ever denounced the treaty. It binds the way an old
debt binds: nobody pays, and nobody burns the paper.

**Of this book, and the purse that made it.** By minute of the Table of Residents, the ninth of
Unicorn, **3502**, this Compendium was commissioned. The proximate cause was a schoolbook: a
northern primer that reached a southern court with three peoples missing and a fourth invented. It
was resolved *that the peoples of Ymir be henceforth represented each by their own account,
gathered at the Seat* — I watched the drafting, and can report that the word traded away in
committee was "misrepresented," which was honester. The task fell to the archive, which is to say
to me. The gathering took sixteen years, and its methods were three: post, supper, and waiting.
Post: eleven years of it for the Gaerwath chapter alone. Supper: nine of them, itemised below.
Waiting: the Ophiur leaf came to us, across three seas, after the book was sewn, and shamed the
waiting into a method. As for the paying — the reader has met "the Compendium's purse" in a note
about a goat, and deserves the accounts behind the joke. The purse is subscribed by the chairs,
each in kind: Letia in paper, the dwarfholds in ink and clasps, Cartosa in carriage under the Blue
Mantles' seal, the Fae in lodging and in a scrutiny no forger has yet survived, the Gnomes in
bookkeeping, exact and unthanked. What follows is the bursar's hand, not mine.

> *From the Compendium's book of accounts (Elendor; in the bursar's hand).* Item, paper of Armora,
> three bales, 61 crowns; ink of Naughrith, nine flasks, 14. Item, carriage of letters to and from
> the Drowned Athenaeum, eleven years, 22 crowns; nota: the consent obtained is entered under no
> column, there being none for it. Item, to the wreckers of the Twin Coasts, salvage of one sealed
> tin, 8 crowns; entered at 8 crowns, being beyond price. Item, to Mistress P. Quondam, nine
> suppers, 3 crowns 4; item, one (1) goat, delivered to the Vorskgen of the cold marches, 2 crowns;
> entered under debts of narrators, discharged. Item, to the copyists, for the margins, nothing:

> quires billed; fifteen bound; the difference is not in these accounts. Item, to the Curator,
> salary: see under ink.

I have queried that last line each year for sixteen years. Master Fennick regards the query as
settled by the entry.

**Last, the house.** My mother's house stands third from the corner where Lantern Row turns up the
hill — a Tall Folk house of the harbour rows, four rooms, boom-chain wages. It has eleven windows.
I knew that before I knew my letters; I counted them from the quay, evenings, and the counting was
a way of holding the house still until my father came off the chain. The crack is over the kitchen
door. It arrived with the Chaos, generations before us, and came with the lease, and my mother
would never have it plastered: a house, she said, should not tell lies about the ground it stands
on. I have tried to make a book on her principle and this chapter to her instruction, and the
instruction is harder. The cracks of my country, counted, are these: we live by being everyone's
harbour, and so belong not quite to ourselves; our parliament's patience is indistinguishable, in a
bad decade, from paralysis; our smallest people carries a hatred the rest of us call charming
because we do not understand it; and under the fairest hall on Ymir four pages are missing that I
have stopped asking after, which is not the same as ceasing to think about. The windows I leave to
the reader. There are a great many; the evening light through them is silver; and I am not able to
be exact about it, which is how the reader may know that I am home.

They tell me this chapter, too, has margins now, in the hand the copyists could not copy. I have
not read them. Everything else in this book I have read twice; but a man may decline to read what
has been written about his mother's house in a script the eye does not like to rest upon. If they
contradict me, trust them. That rule of mine survives its author — and I find, setting it down,
that I have nothing further to add; which, in a writer of notes, the reader may take for peace.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *He has read these margins. He reads everything twice; it is the best thing about him, and he has
> just told you the second-best: trust them. So let one margin in this book stand with nothing to
> correct. I have annotated every teller in these pages for the seam in their sincerity; this one I
> annotate for the record. The chapter is honest. The cracks are counted, and the count is true —
> and the windows, Curator, were also worth the counting. The house stands.*
