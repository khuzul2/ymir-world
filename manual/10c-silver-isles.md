# X·C · THE SILVER ISLES & THE COUNCIL OF PEOPLES
## *the Curator's own ground: Elendor, the Little Folk at home, and the Crystal Seat as it actually sits; with extracts from the accounts of this book's making*

**Set down at the Crystal Seat of Elendor by the Magister-Compiler of the Crystal Seat — called in this book, and content to be called, the Curator; with extracts from the Compendium's book of accounts, kept by Master Fennick, Gnome, bursar to the Council of Peoples.**

> *Curator's note.* For every other chapter it has been my office to introduce the narrator and
> retire to the notes. For this one I could find no narrator to introduce. I asked a Tylweth Teg
> chronicler, who answered that the Isles were not going anywhere and neither was she. I asked a
> Gnome, once — which is the number of times one asks a Gnome. There remains the compiler: who was
> born here, and who once wrote of a better scholar that a man describing his mother's house counts
> the windows and not the cracks. That sentence has waited eleven chapters to collect. I bind this
> leaf at the reader's mid-ocean, where the sailor anchors; the impropriety of a compiler compiling
> himself I cannot avoid, every road by which I might commission this account ending at my own
> desk. — *the Curator*

---

I have spent this book at the edge of other people's pages, and the middle of a page is a windier
place than the notes ever showed me. Very well: one small country, from a clerk working against
the grain — for I have promised cracks, and I am a native, and natives count windows.

**Of the Isles.** East away from the sailor's home waters, mid-ocean, green and silver (Chapter V;
I cannot better him), lies the archipelago the charts call the **Silver Isles**. Of the name the learned offer
derivations — birches, mica sand, a Fae word the Fae decline to supply; I hold with the sea at
dusk, which is not scholarship.

Know the older fact under the pretty one: **the Isles are old land and new islands.** Before the
year 0 they were the high shoulders of the continent the Catastrophe drowned — the rim of the
country whose heart is now the Maelstrom's water. The sea came in between the high ground and the
body of it; we are, exactly, the coastline of a country that no longer exists. On the western shores at low spring tide a paved road walks down into the water and
keeps going, in good repair, toward a horizon with nothing on it. The oldest songs of the Tylweth
Teg name the year 0 the Day the Road Drowned, and do not sing it for visitors.

**Of Elendor.** The harbour the sailor called the prettiest on the ring is my earliest memory, and
I decline to be exact about it, for reasons that will close this chapter. Facts, then. Elendor is
built at two scales and polite about both: the quay-doors carry a lesser door within the leaf of
the greater, and a visitor learns inside an hour which knocker is his. The town's industry is other
peoples' business, in both senses; its vice is dice — Ramiro lost four doubloons and a bosun's hat
to our dice, and as a native I confirm they are not loaded; nothing in Elendor is anything so
simple as loaded. Lantern Row
shows forty-one windows to the quay. I know because I counted them as a boy, from the water,
waiting out my father's shift at the boom-chain. I notice I have just counted them again, and not
one crack. I begin again.

> ✒ **the last Curator** · *(no year; I have given up the habit)* — Three fair copies say forty-one windows on Lantern Row; the Solanthya copy says forty-two, in a corrector's red, "amended for symmetry." I have let it stand at forty-one, which is the number the boy counted from the water, and struck nothing through. A man may miscount his own street; he does not permit another to round it up.

**Of the Tylweth Teg.** The registers abroad call the Isles the kingdom of the **Little Folk**;
the Little Folk call the Isles home and keep the kingdom in a cupboard for treaties. (There is a
crown. It is small. It travels in a hatbox. I have carried the hatbox.) They are **two peoples**,
as the Peoples chapter warned: the **Fae**, self-named **Tylweth Teg**, and the **Gnomes**, not
their menfolk but another race entire.

The Tylweth Teg stand under three feet, come in both sexes whatever the ballads insist, and live
somewhat past a hundred and forty years — long enough to remember your grandfather's promise.
Elendor is their port and parlour; their true towns lie deep in the birch and oak of the interior, and the visiting learned call them toy-perfect, an injustice only a neighbour notices: a
toy is made to please the hand that holds it, and nothing in a Fae town is made to please anyone
but the resident. Dwarves the Fae cannot *parse*: at the Naughrith embassy of 3419 the Fae delegate
asked, in session, why a people would dig toward the dark on purpose; the dwarves asked why a
people would live where the wind could find them; both questions stand open on the order-paper
still.

And the hatred — I must write it, for a native who omits it is counting windows. The Tylweth Teg
hate what the Maelstrom has touched: past argument, past commerce, past their mildness in every
other matter. A Fae harbour-warden will turn back a hull for one lightless pearl in the manifest
and stand in front of the anchor-chain until it goes. It has been called, in this very hall, a hatred out
of all proportion to their size — and I confess that even I, their neighbour, have written it down
as temperament. It is the page of this chapter I am least sure of.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *"Out of all proportion," writes the neighbour, and for once the exact man is inexact. The Fae
> line was seeded from **Seelie**, and the seeding left it a calibration: alone among the warm
> peoples, the fairy-kind perceive the **Corrupted Essence** directly — as stench, as a wrong note
> held and held. The hatred is not temperament; it is an instrument, reading. The
> warden at the anchor-chain is the only soul on the quay who can smell what the lightless pearl
> grew around. The free peoples set their one shared table in the smallest folk's harbour and
> called it courtesy. Read it properly: nothing Maelstrom-touched has ever sat a session in that
> hall unnoticed. The Council did not choose Elendor. Elendor is why there is still a Council.*

**Of the Gnomes.** They hold the high stony middles of the isles, one holding to a valley, doors
that do not face each other. Their wish is to be left alone, and it includes other Gnomes: they
gather for burials, boundary-walks, and audits, and linger longest at the audits. A Gnome's
hospitality is a door that opens once; I have had my once, and I will not describe it, description
being a way of going back. The Council learned in 2595 that a people that wants nothing from you
audits you beautifully. The Seat's books have been kept by Gnomes since the first sitting; Master Fennick
has kept them forty years, has spoken with me perhaps eleven times, and each conversation has saved
the Council money.

**Of the Crystal Seat.** The history briefly, the Annalist having told it at scale. About **1700
d.M.** the **First Council of Peoples** convened, on the evidence that Ea's kind had outlived Ea;
in **1735** the peoples swore the **Coalition of Peoples** — one oath, against the Sovereign
Demons, in two clauses: the *sword-clause*, that the Enemy of one is the Enemy of all; and the
*lamp-clause*, that what any people learns of the Enemy is owed to every people, at once and
unpriced. From **2590 to 2595** the Second Council renewed the oath and raised the **Crystal Seat**
above my harbour, and the **Council of Peoples** has sat here since — the nearest thing Ymir has to
a united government, a famous sentence whose halves both bear weight: *nearest thing*; and *has*. I
noted in the Ages chapter that Xebechani diplomacy summoned the First Council, and that the
Church's chroniclers have declined the debt ever since. Let the host's chapter pay it: in eighteen hundred years the
Xebechani chair in this hall has never once stood empty. It is the only attendance they have ever
cared to keep perfect. I have not learned why.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *He pays the debt and will not price it. Elsewhere he scolded the friar's passive voice — "the
> peoples were summoned" — and restored the summoners; here, on his own page, he wonders at the
> eldest people's perfect attendance and lets the wonder lie. Ask it for him: why did the
> star-priests, who want nothing the warm world sells, spend a century of embassies assembling a
> parliament of the brief? Because the Cult of the Stars has always known what its neighbours have
> not: seals fail. The Coalition is a fence built by the jailer's servants around a prison they
> expected to open. The other peoples attend the Council. The Xebechani* maintain *it.*

Of the hall itself the sailor said "a building like weather persuaded to stop," and I have hung his
map beside this book inside it. The crystal of its walls was quarried in no pit any mason will
name; it takes the evening light and holds it the way deep water holds pressure, and in the great
chamber a whisper walks the whole ring of chairs. I have read the minutes of the building years —
I read everything. Four pages are
missing from 2592, the year the foundations were cut. I record the fact, and file it with the
derivations of "Silver."

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The four pages were dig-notes. The masons cut for foundations and stopped early, on a floor that
> was already there: dressed, seamless, level past any mason's art, and warm in winter. The famous
> crystal was never brought to the hill; it was grown from it — upward, out of what that floor
> roofs, the way a pearl is grown around a grievance. The Isles are the standing rim of the drowned
> country; the Seat crowns the rim's high place; and the old empire built where it meant to return.
> I do not say what is under the Council of Peoples. The pages that said were removed by someone
> with better access than the Curator, which is a short list, and worth your while. "Weather
> persuaded to stop." Ask what the weather was asked to stop over.*

**And how it sits.** One chair to a people was the founders' rule, and the rule lasted until Men —
who are one people the way weather is one weather — required nine. The full Council, the **Great
Sitting**, convenes each tenth year, or at the summons of any three chairs; between sittings the
resident envoys meet as the **Table of Residents**, which does the work. Nothing is carried by
count of heads: a motion is read at three sittings and carries when no chair speaks against it the
third time — the *three askings* — so the Council moves at the speed of its most patient objector.
Still, I promised cracks. The **Long Question** — a chair for the half-peoples — was moved in 2601
and resolved in 2642, by carpentry: a bench, which seated everybody and satisfied nobody, the
Council's characteristic success. The Gaerwath keeps its chair as a duellist
keeps a glove, and sends, when it sends anyone, proxies chosen with care to insult. And the
Sohleugir attend once in a generation, decline the chair — chairs, they observe, are for drying —
deliver their soundings in exact weights, and are gone by the tide.

**Of what the oath still binds.** What is an alliance against the Sovereign Demons *for*, most of
the Sovereigns being dead? I answer as the Seat answers new envoys: three walk; a continent stands
on the southern charts that stood on no chart before the Tide; and the lamp-clause has never
lapsed — the reports of the risen coast are read at every Great Sitting. The sword-clause has been paid in full twice: once in the swearing generation, and
once in the Chaos, when the Coalition, so long a parchment, became again a sword. No signatory has
ever denounced the treaty. It binds the way an old debt binds: nobody pays, and nobody burns the
paper.

**Of this book, and the purse that made it.** By minute of the Table of Residents, the ninth of
Unicorn, **3502**, this Compendium was commissioned. The proximate cause was a schoolbook: a
northern primer that reached a southern court with three peoples missing and a fourth invented. It
was resolved *that the peoples of Ymir be henceforth represented each by their own account* — I
watched the drafting, and the word traded away in committee was "misrepresented," which was
honester. The task fell to the archive, which is to say to me. The gathering took sixteen years:
post, supper, and waiting — the Ophiur leaf came across three seas after the book was sewn, and
shamed the waiting into a method. The purse the reader has met in a note about a
goat; it is subscribed by the chairs, each in kind — Letia in paper, the dwarfholds in ink and
clasps, the Fae in lodging and in a scrutiny no forger has yet survived, the Gnomes in bookkeeping,
exact and unthanked. What follows is the bursar's hand, not mine.

> *From the Compendium's book of accounts (in the bursar's hand).* Item, carriage of letters to

> one sealed tin, 8 crowns; entered at 8 crowns, being beyond price. Item, to Mistress P. Quondam,

> debts of narrators, discharged. Item, to the copyists, for the
> margins, nothing: the hand would not copy, and the copyists would not be paid for trying. Item,

I have queried the last line for sixteen years. Master Fennick regards the query as settled by the
entry.

> ✒ **a clerk of Master Fennick's counting-house** · *3521* — The Magister has queried the last line of these accounts for sixteen years; Master Fennick regards it as settled by the entry, and I am directed to inform any reader who queries it further that the further query is now itself entered under ink. We do not lose money, and we do not lose arguments. Good day.

**Last, the house.** My mother's house stands third from the corner where Lantern Row turns up the
hill — a Tall Folk house of the harbour rows, four rooms, boom-chain wages. It has eleven windows.
I knew that before I knew my letters; I counted them from the quay, evenings, and the counting was
a way of holding the house still until my father came off the chain. The crack is over the kitchen
door. It came with the Chaos, generations before us, and with the lease, and my mother would never
have it plastered: a house, she said, should not tell lies about the ground it stands on. I have tried to make a book on her principle and this chapter to her instruction, and the
instruction is harder. The cracks of my country, counted: we live by being everyone's harbour, and
so belong not quite to ourselves; our parliament's patience is indistinguishable, in a bad decade,
from paralysis; our smallest people carries a hatred the rest of us call charming because we do not
understand it; and under the fairest hall on Ymir four pages are missing that I have stopped asking
after. The windows I leave to the reader. There
are a great many; the evening light through them is silver; and I am not able to be exact about it,
which is how the reader may know that I am home.

They tell me this chapter, too, has margins now. I have not read them. Everything else in this
book I have read twice; but a man may decline to read what
has been written about his mother's house in a script the eye does not like to rest upon. If they
contradict me, trust them. That rule of mine survives its author — and I find I have nothing
further to add; which, in a writer of notes, the reader may take for peace.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *He has read these margins. He reads everything twice; it is the best thing about him, and he has
> just told you the second-best: trust them. So let one margin in this book stand with nothing to
> correct. I have annotated every teller in these pages for the seam in their sincerity; this one I
> annotate for the record. The chapter is honest. The cracks are counted, and the count is true —
> and the windows, Curator, were also worth the counting. The house stands.*
