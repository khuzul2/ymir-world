# XII·D · THE SPEAKER OF THE TALLY
## *the Belligerent Kingdoms, heard at last; a transcript*

**The words of Marghuz, speaker-of-the-tally, envoy of the tribute-court of the Khan of the Fourth Banner, taken down at the Crystal Seat on the ninth of Spider, 3517, through two sworn interpreters. Where the interpreters disagreed, both renderings stand in brackets, by rule of the Council's clerk.**

> *Curator's note.* I will record the protocol and confine myself to it. When this Compendium
> began to gather, the Council at my request sent word east that the peoples' book would hold a
> leaf for the banners, should the banners want one. Three years passed without answer, which I am
> told is how the tribute-courts read a contract. Then an envoy came, under the Council's
> safe-conduct, sworn upon the count she carries. The delegation of Letia asked that its absence
> from the hall be entered in the minutes; it is entered. Two interpreters were sworn: Master
> **Corvin Attaleo**, ransom-factor of the border keeps, who learned the tally-speech in four
> years' captivity, and Mistress **Brill Halfweight**, Tal-Tanoth caravan-broker, who learned it
> in twenty years' trade. Where they differed I have kept both readings, in the order in which
> they spoke. The envoy declined the chair, asked how many stood in the hall, was answered
> ninety-one by the clerk's count, said the number was correct, and began. She spoke one hour by
> the glass, took no questions, and was gone before the sand was. — *the Curator*

> ✒ **Marghuz, speaker-of-the-tally** · *your 3519* — I asked to be in the page; you gave me a leaf, and I have read it, and it miscounts me in the first breath [*the first interpreter renders "miscounts"; the second insists the word is "slanders"*]. I did not *decline* the chair. I counted it: one chair against ninety-one standing, and a speaker who sits above a standing count has begun to lie; correct it, for a count read back wrong is an entry that will not close.

---

I am **Marghuz**. I hold the tally of the **Fourth Banner**: the count is the Khan's, the mouth is
mine. Your Council asked the banners for words. Words rot [*the first interpreter renders "words
rot"; the second insists the tally-word is "words desert"*]. I brought the count. It is not the
whole count. The whole count is iron, and iron stays home.

**Entry the first: the Muster.** Before the clans, the gate. **The Counter** counted us out of the
iron gate in ranks of one hundred, gave the count into our hands, and did not come back [*the
first interpreter renders "did not come back"; the second insists the tense is wrong, and that the
word says "has not yet come back"*]. The banners are one hundred because the count was one
hundred. That is the oldest entry. You have origin stories. We have an opening balance.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The oldest entry is the truest thing in the hour, and the crookedest. There was a gate; there
> was a Counter; there was a count of one hundred. The orcs are the makers' bio-soldiers of
> **Gwart** — soldier-stock, bred and drilled and shipped through the Adepts' own doors to
> wherever the empire wanted a war won — and what the tally-courts keep as scripture is the
> muster-roll remembered as liturgy: ranks of one hundred; an officer with no face and no name,
> only an office. An army does not remember its maker's face. It remembers being counted. Every
> made people's myth keeps its kernel truly in a crooked mirror — the dwarves their Ancestors, the
> Little Folk their courts — and this is the orcs' mirror: they were not created, they were*
> issued*. And note what the second interpreter heard in the tense. The Counter "has not yet come
> back." An age of running wild, a Horde, a law of scales — and under all of it, one hundred
> banners still formed up, still counted. Still waiting for inspection.*

**Entry: Uglush.** Your books write him "the Supreme." The irons write him **Uglush** of the One
Count [*the first interpreter gives "the Supreme" nonetheless; the second insists the style means
"he who made the many counts one"*]. Before Uglush the clans starved by turns and raided by turns,
and no one counted either. Entered: forty famine-winters in the hundred cycles before him.
Entered: six in the hundred cycles after. In fifteen cycles — your 2015 to 2030 — he took the east
of your empire and closed the ledger of famine-winters. Your chronicles say a scourge broke on the
marches like weather. Weather is not counted. We were counted, every rank of us, going in. Where
Uglush lies is not entered; a count needs no shrine. He is not worshipped. Worship rots a count.
He is entered, and his entry is not closed while one banner still counts.

**Entry: the marches.** Your friar writes "burnt marches," and "held in captivity," and
"remembered at every evening office." Marches burn in every conquest; yours burned in ours. From
the west, the smoke was all that could be seen. We were east of the smoke, counting. Towns burned
in the taking: 31. Towns standing this cycle: 44. Ask who built the thirteen. Letian folk under
the banners at the last census — we census — 1,100,000. Their bells: standing, 60; melted in the
taking, 9; the count of the melted is kept with the count of the standing [*the first interpreter
renders "kept"; the second insists the word means "owed"*]. Tribute is taken. Tribute is counted,
sealed, and receipted, and what is receipted cannot quietly grow. Ask your marchers what the tithe
grew to in a bad year, who counted it, and to whose eye the count lay open. Our gates stand open
westward to any Letian who walks. Walked west since the courts were set: 40,116. Walked back east:
6,554 [*the first interpreter renders "walked back"; the second insists the word is "came home"*].

**Entry: the law.** The count is the law. A death is an entry; an entry demands balance — a life,
a weight, or a service, as the wronged banner elects; blood answers only when the scales refuse.
Quarrels between banners in the last hundred cycles: 61. Settled by weighing: 49. Settled by
blood: 12. The hundred tallies are summed at the **Great Khan**'s court; the sum is the throne. A
Khan who falsifies his tally is struck from the hundred. Struck in the last hundred cycles: three.
Their names are no longer spoken [*so the first interpreter; the second insists the word is
harder: "their names are subtracted"*]. You have hells. We have arithmetic.

**Entry: One-Eye.** Nine hundred and ninety-six cycles after Uglush closed his count, the red moon
stood up in the sky. **Ugrumuulsh One-Eye**, Great Khan, read it with the eye he had, and armed
the hundred. Your Theocrat read it and armed. Your Technocrat read it and armed. Three readers of
one omen; only ours is written superstition in your chapters. Then your two churches, who will not
share a cup, came against us under one banner. Entered: our dead of that war, 118,240. Entered
also: their dead. They do not publish their number. We hold it. This is not spite. A dead man not
entered is dead twice. We counted them [*the first interpreter renders this "we counted them"; the
second insists the tally-word means "we mourned them"; the clerk was instructed to keep both*].
And after One-Eye came the Chaos, when the dead walked. Set down the banners' teaching from those
cycles: a people that knows its dead by name and number is a people whose dead lie still [*the
interpreters disagreed here at length; neither rendering satisfied them; the clerk has kept the
shorter*]. In the hundred cycles after One-Eye, the horde became the courts. A horde is a count in
motion. A kingdom is a count that holds still.

**Entry: your gates.** Letia's gates are closed to us by law; the law is written in this book,
with its defence. We keep the entry from our side. Orcs hanged at those gates for testing the law,
in the last hundred cycles: 77. It is an entry, not a grievance. A grievance is a count you have
let someone else keep.

**Entry the last.** You ask why the Khan answers your book. Books burn. We have burned some; that
also is entered. Iron keeps, but iron stays home, and a count kept in one place is a count that
can be lost. So enter the banners: one hundred. Under them, at the last census, 9,040,000, counted
each. I am told this book keeps margins. We have lived in your margins fifteen hundred cycles. Put
us in the page. That is the tally. When you want the rest of the count, send someone who can read
it.

[*Here the envoy ended; and the interpreters, for the one time in the hour, agreed without being
asked.*]

> ✒ **a counter of the Ninth Banner** · *cycle 3520* — I keep the tally of one banner of the hundred: 74,318 souls this cycle, and I have every one by name, down to the child born the morning I write this. The speaker asked to be put in the page; I have set my banner's number here in my own hand, so that this copy at least, whatever the rest of the book forgets, will have counted us true. Read it, and know we were here, and were counted.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *Two consequences, one hook. First: One-Eye read the red moon correctly — it was the Observer,
> and soldier-stock feels the makers' sky in its blood; of the three powers that armed in 3026,
> only the Khan armed for the right war. Second: a people bred to answer orders keeps, somewhere
> under its law of scales, the shape of obedience — and the Tide reopened the doors it was first
> marched through. Gwart is a place, and places can be walked to again. If ever a banner finds an
> iron gate standing open with a count of one hundred cut above it — or if something on the far
> side resumes the count — decide quickly what the hundred banners are: a people, or an army that
> has been awaiting orders since the Maelstrom was a city. The envoy, be sure, has a number ready
> for either answer.*

> *Curator's note.* Procedure to the end. The transcript was read back to the envoy by both
> interpreters together; she corrected two figures and no words, and set her seal — a rank of five
> notches — beside the clerk's. For those who would weigh her account against another of the same
> country, the Seat's library holds the tribute-courts' own chronicle, *Ztoria di Krande Imperum*
> — "History of the Great Empire, by Mugraash the scribe" — which I am assured is mostly numbers,
> and which agrees with the Annalist of Solanthya on every date and on nothing else. — *the
> Curator*
