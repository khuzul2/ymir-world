# XIV·B · FIGURES OF RENOWN
## *being, in Part One, extracts of the Register of Figures of Renown kept by the Letian Inquisition, with the Verdicts of the Holy Office; and in Part Two, the Companies of the Turning Age, wherein the narrators of this book name the adventurers of their own lands*

**As compiled for the use of the Inquisition of the Clericate of Mithra by Frater Remigius Falx, Canon-Historian of the Holy Office, in the registry at Solanthya, in the year of the Maelstrom 3518, under the present High Theocrat, whom Mithra preserve.**

> *Curator's note.* The Inquisition of Letia keeps a register of the world's renowned, so that its
> examiners may know by whom the folk swear before hearing how they pray. It is recopied each
> generation and the superseded copy burned; the under-censor charged with the last burning
> retired instead — suddenly, and to Cartosa — and this is the copy he did not burn, bought at two
> removes. I have bound it behind the huntsman's book of beasts, it being a bestiary of another
> kind. One caution before the verdicts, reader: they are measurements of piety, not of truth, and
> the instrument is calibrated at Solanthya. — *the Curator*

# PART ONE · THE REGISTER OF THE HOLY OFFICE
## *the renowned of the ancient and settled world, as the Letian Inquisition files them*

To the Reverend Fathers Examiners; and to my successor upon this shelf, whomever Mithra afflicts
with the appointment.

Renown is a road, and roads carry pilgrims or plague; the Office must know the traffic. The
common man swears by these names, christens his sons with them, and takes doctrine from a dead
hero sooner than from a living homilist. Hence this Register:
first what the charters attest; then what common report adds, marked as report; last the Verdict
of the Office, which is the purpose of the exercise. And let my successor mark it early: the
Office burns less than the world believes. The lowest shelves are locked, not empty. What follows
is the shelf we show. The entries stand in the order of their floruit, so far as the calendars of
the fallen world allow; where the elves are concerned no order was obtainable, and I have ceased
to ask.

**I. Topramesk, called the Yellow, and by the charitable the Mad** — archmage of the last Adepts;
the Ancient Days. The first name in the Register and the oldest file in the house. He led the
remnant of the sorcerers' empire into the Academy at the heart of fallen Ragash and wrought the
ritual that ended the Demon Wars — and the land with them: where the Splendent City stood, the
Maelstrom turns to this day. The doctors read the Catastrophe as judgement upon pride; the Office
sees no cause to distinguish the sentence from the culprit's handiwork. Report in the east will
have it that he is not done dying. Report is not evidence.
*Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMER OF THE FIRST MAGNITUDE; author, under Heaven, of the world's
wound. He predates the Office and every court but one. The file remains open, on principle.

**II. Inanna, called the Splendent** — queen of the Light Elves; her date beyond our reckoning.
The legendary queen of Alfheim, who in the deeps of elven time drove the demon-binding Dark Elves
from that realm into their Gaerwath exile, where they remain, and remain a lesson. Her line ruled
the Shining City until the Serpent unmade it in the Tide, and a queen of her blood replants the
splinter now. The exiles curse her house and lay their fall from paradise upon her ancestor —
which is, the Office observes, what exiles always say.
*Verdict of the Office:* BEYOND THE WRIT — an elf, and no subject of Mithra's law; her war upon
the demonolaters is nonetheless entered with an approval the Office is not, strictly, entitled to
feel.

**III. Aslund, called the Swordless** — legionary of the Tenth; Helgedad, in the Empire's day.
Builder of the wall that bears his name: four hundred kilometres of stone from sea to sea across
the Kelmark, raised against the northern barbarians while Helgedad was yet of the Empire — the
year itself the northern rolls do not condescend to give. The north says he laid his own sword in
the foundation-course, whence the byname. The Office has no doctrine of buried swords; it has a
doctrine of works, and the Wall of Aslund is the largest single prayer in the world's north.
*Verdict of the Office:* PIOUS, after the frontier fashion; a wall being a psalm in stone, he is
entered among the devout — the more readily as he can no longer be examined on particulars.

**IV. Uglush, called the Supreme** — warlord of the Great Orc Horde; 2015–2030 d.M. The scourge of
the east. In fifteen years of fire his Horde carved from the Empire's living body the realms now
called Belligerent, where the Khans squabble upon Letian bones. Of his end the chronicles are
silent, orcs keeping no registry that Mithra would audit. Anathema was pronounced while the
marches still burned and is renewed at every synod since; my predecessors note, with the honesty
this Register requires, that the anathema has to date recovered no province.
*Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMER; ANATHEMA, pronounced and perpetually renewed. See the
caution at entry X below, and let no novice merge the files.

**V. Morannagul, styled the Eternal** — unifier of the Gaerwath; 2550 d.M. to this present
writing. In 2550 a young Dark-Elf lord unified the Gaerwath; in 3518 the same lord rules it
still, unaged, over an empire that keeps our kind for slaves and binds demons for statecraft.
This registry holds nine hundred years of findings against him; he has outlived every hand that
wrote them, and the anathema, renewed by Theocrat after Theocrat, he is nowhere reported to have
noticed.
*Verdict of the Office:* DEMONOLATER; ANATHEMA, many times renewed and demonstrably not
inconvenienced. The Office persists. So, regrettably, does he.

**VI. Valdis Nemertine** — last heir of the throne of Letia; fell 2563 d.M. In that year the
Sovereign Demon Saxul, Lord of All Fevers and Pestilence, manifested in the imperial capital, and
at the Battle of the Broken Land was destroyed — the second of his kind ever slain — Valdis
giving her life in the destroying. The crown ascended with her; the crozier, in God's season,
took up what the crown had laid down. Her cause of sainthood is the largest file in this
registry. Of the Sword of Valdis: three shrines display a sword apiece; the Office has
authenticated none and — the pilgrims being devout and the offerings regular — has closed none.
*Verdict of the Office:* MARTYRDOM CONFIRMED; SAINTHOOD PENDING, these nine hundred and fifty
years. Heaven is not slow. Heaven is thorough.

**VII. The house of Carrillo** — princes of Cartosa; 1906 d.M. to this present. The princely line
of the daughter-colony since a governor was raised to Prince in 1906. Of their deeds: Rodriguez
XI reduced Meridian and annexed it in the War of the Red Sea, 2817–2830; Alberto V took the
throne in the Year of the Black Sun, 3017, having — so every southern telling insists — held his
own palace stair against the Black Dawn. Cartosa's heralds cannot even agree in which years the
thirteenth Rodriguez reigned and the fifth Alberto succeeded; the Office files both accounts and
trusts neither. The house tithes chiefly to Orum, invokes Mithra at coronations, and remits to
Solanthya with commendable punctuality.
*Verdict of the Office:* LAX, the line entire — the sea-god at table, the sun-god on feast-days;
funds however accepted.

**VIII. Berthrant Vaelaer** — champion; Helgedad, 3015–3020 d.M. When the army of the Wolves took
Nortislong in a single day and was as suddenly gone, it was Berthrant Vaelaer who would not let
the leavings live: five years he hunted the remnant, sworn to scour Evil from that island root
and stone, with a muster at his back that the north still sings. Helgedad left the Empire's fold
in 2600, and its creeds are Thurms's and colder; the man himself is not recorded to have spared
one servant of the Dark in five winters, which is more orthodoxy than several archbishops have
shown.
*Verdict of the Office:* SCHISMATIC BY GEOGRAPHY; RIGHTEOUS BY WORKS. Entered as PIOUS, if
lamentably armed, and lamentably elsewhere.

**IX. Tobias** — captain of the Black Dawn; Cartosa, 3017 d.M. Master of the cult called the
Black Dawn — Ahriman's, and the Sovereign Demons', its seal a sun eclipsed — and author of the
failed coup that gave the Year of the Black Sun its name. The coup broke; the cult burned. The
Office's experience of burned cults may be summarised: cults do not burn, they cure, like ill-cut
timber. No body was produced, and the Register does not close a demonolater's file on the
strength of smoke.
*Verdict of the Office:* DEMONOLATER; UNREPENTANT, twice invited. The invitations stand
cancelled. The watch does not.

**X. Ugrumuulsh, called One-Eye** — Great Khan of the orcs; fl. 3026 d.M. Great Khan of the
Belligerent Kingdoms in the year the red moon rose, when he read the omen as the Theocrat and the
Technocrat read it, and armed as they armed — the single recorded instance of doctrinal agreement
between Solanthya and an orc. CAUTION FOR EXAMINERS, in my own hand and underscored: this is not
Uglush the Supreme, who burned the east a thousand years before him. Two warlords; two files; one
smell. The files have been merged three times by novices and unmerged three times by me.
*Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMER, in his own right and not his predecessor's. The distinction
is doctrine. See that it stays seen.

**XII. Obnubia** — a seer; the years of the Tide. A seer of the Tide's eve, whose sight — common
report insists, and sober captains confirm — found true where the Obscuring should have blinded
her. Doctrine is settled that no honest divination reaches far under the Maelstrom; Obnubia's did
not trouble to be dishonest, merely correct. The Register records, because it must, that the
Office itself put questions to her twice in that generation. The answers are filed on the lowest
shelf, and both came true, which is why the shelf is the lowest.
*Verdict of the Office:* NEITHER PIOUS NOR IMPIOUS BUT INCONVENIENT; not to be consulted;
consulted twice; see the previous clause, and do not see it again.

**XIII. Tofango** — axeman; bearer of Oblivion; the years of the Tide. The greatest axeman of his

hermit upon it. The body was found. The axe was not. The colleges teach his case in a southern
doctor's words: corruption is of the instrument and the handling, not of the man. Our theologians
disputed forty years whether such affliction is sin, and concluded, for once, in mercy.
*Verdict of the Office:* AFFLICTED, NOT APOSTATE; his madness absolved, his axe not. The axe
being at large, the mercy is under review.

absolved daily, in every pulpit, according to the preacher. The Office waits for a defendant. It
has waited four hundred and ninety years. The Office is patient.

## § A SUPPLEMENT, ENTERED OUT OF ITS SEASON
### *the Adepts, and certain names lately come to the Office from a seized catalogue of the heathen*

**A supplement, and a caution folded into it.** Since the extracts above were fair-copied, the
Office has taken into the house a thing it long lacked and never wanted: a heathen **catalogue of
the Adepts**, compiled in the deep past by one **Tarogald of Mileus** in six-and-twenty books he
titled *De Daemonomachia*, the most of them lost, and glossed since by a living hand that has hunted
the remainder. It names the sorcerer-lords of the world-before as *individuals* — and worse, as
*gods*. By their floruit these names stand at the very head of this Register, before Topramesk
himself; but a roll the whole house cites by its numbers is not to be re-sorted for a heretic's
bookkeeping, and I will not have the first entries renumbered and five centuries of cross-references
broken to flatter a pagan chronology. I enter them here, at the tail, out of their season, and I
command my successor as I commanded him at entry X: **do not merge them forward.** Let them sit
apart, as the thing they describe set itself apart — above the world, and answerable to none of it.

**XXI. Ört di Xand, called the Shadowed** — an Adept; the Ancient Days, before reckoning. The Fifth
of the sorcerer-lords the catalogue numbers, and by its account a *god-king*: ruler of the
south-eastern marches of the land the old texts name **Mandolia**, in the reaches of the Sadhir and
Ophiur, in an age the fallen calendars keep by a "**Sun of the Golden Dragon**" this Office does not
reckon. He is said to have wrought a **Gem** — a working, the catalogue insists, and not a stone —
able to summon and command seven stars of some heathen sky; and, that labour done, to have
"**ascended into the sky**," which the doctors of Mithra read as the eldest blasphemy a creature can
commit: a man who made himself a god. Report gives his pupil vanished in the northern mists; report
is this catalogue's whole substance, and the catalogue is a heathen's.
*Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMER OF THE FIRST MAGNITUDE, and of the first *kind* — not a man who
knelt to a false god but a man who *decreed himself* one; ANATHEMA upon the name, though the name
predates the Office, the Empire, and the Church, and every court that might have tried it save the
one that broke the world. The file is opened at the head of all guilt, and kept, on principle,
without end.

**XXII. Svelos** — an Adept; the Ancient Days. Set down in the same catalogue among the greatest of
the sorcerer-lords: the Adept who, the heathen say, stood at Topramesk's side to drive back the
demon **Gol Sauthaur** in the Demon Wars, and who bound his arts into a book the tellers call the
**Iron Tome**. That he warred upon demons the Office does not doubt; that he did it as a self-made
god it does not pardon, the *good* end of a blasphemer being the most dangerous kind. His pupil
Taros follows.
*Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMER OF THE FIRST MAGNITUDE; a defender of the world who held the
world in a usurper's hand. ANATHEMA. The Office notes, and buries in the noting, that the age owes
him a battle: a debt to a false god is discharged in his fall, never in his honour.

**XXIII. Taros** — pupil of Svelos; the Ancient Days. Of him the catalogue keeps little but his
master's name and a vault — the **Halls of Taros**, said to lie in the southern reach beneath a peak
called **Ulmar**, where he laid up his works and his dead behind runes and the signs of ancient
kings. The relic-hunters of the south pursue it yet; the Office pursues the relic-hunters.
*Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMER, as his master and his master's whole breed; DISCIPLE, which in
this matter is neither excuse nor mitigation. Watch entered upon his vault — and upon any examiner
who is found to have developed an interest in its door.

**XXIV. King Teran X of Cartosa** — a tyrant of legend; the southern seas, the century disputed. The

Cartosa crowned no kings: it kept governors, and then the princes of the house of **Carrillo**

this one sends living men south after an Adept's relic, which is why it is filed and not merely
laughed at.
*Verdict of the Office:* APOCRYPHAL; NO SUCH SOVEREIGN ATTESTED — struck from the regnal reckoning
of Cartosa, in which he was never entered to begin with. Filed under *tales that send men to their
deaths*, a drawer the Office keeps larger than it would wish.

**XXV. Tarogald of Mileus** — a historian; the deep past. Author of the *De Daemonomachia*, the
six-and-twenty books, the most of them lost, from which nearly all the Office now holds of the
Adepts-as-persons is drawn. A pagan chronicler of a pagan age, whose sober account of the demon-wars
the doctors credit, and whose reverence for the sorcerer-lords they do not. He is dead these ages
and cannot be examined, which the Office counts, in a chronicler, among his merits.
*Verdict of the Office:* HEATHEN; UNBAPTISED; and — the register sets it down with distaste —
ACCURATE, a combination the Office finds more troubling than plain error. Cited as an authority;
canonised as nothing.

**XXVI. Loregar** — a treasure-hunter; lately, and not of the ancient roll but entered beside it. A
dying seeker of the Halls of Taros, whose testament — a route, an **amulet** left with one **Belthan**
at a temple of Ishtar, and a confession that he meant to go back and did not — passes among the
relic-hunters of the south the way a map passes, which is to say fatally. The Office holds no charge
against a man for dying poor and curious. It holds his leaf against everyone who reads it.
*Verdict of the Office:* NEITHER PIOUS NOR IMPIOUS, BUT DANGEROUS TO OTHERS; the man beyond
judgement, the leaf not. Suppression of the leaf ordered; the leaf, like every such, copied faster
than it is burned.

Here end the extracts. My successor will add names; the world does not stop minting them. Let him
keep the files distinct, the verdicts dry, and the lowest shelf locked; and let him remember that
our duty is not to admire the light of renown but to record what it consumes. Mithra keep the
Register; the Register, God willing, will help keep Mithra's house.

# PART TWO · THE COMPANIES OF THE TURNING AGE
## *being an account of the adventuring bands whose hands turned the age, gathered by the Curator from the narrators of this book*

> *The Curator resumes.* Here Frater Falx's extracts end, and here I must confess the register's
> quiet failure — which is not the frater's fault, but his office's. The Inquisition of Letia keeps
> the renowned that Letia can see: the ancient, the sainted, the anathematised, the safely dead. But
> the age we live in was not turned by the safely dead. It was turned, in the space of a single
> troubled decade about the Tide, by *companies* — bands of the living and the reckless, who gathered
> the Relics, opened the gate at Ulan-Tang, broke a fever-demon, sailed after a pirate's ghost — and
> of these the frater's register holds only splinters: a bearer here, a heresiarch there, and at the
> hinge of the whole age a ruled blank space.
>
> So I did what a Letian register could not. I asked the narrators of this book — the ones you have
> already learned to read — to name the companies of their own countries, each in the voice you know
> them by; and where the Office deigned to rule upon one of these figures, I have let Frater Falx's
> verdict stand beside the telling, that you may watch renown measured twice over: once by those who
> loved it, and once by those who policed it. What follows is not tidy. Companies are not tidy. But
> read here, and you will see the turning age from the inside of the hands that turned it. — *the
> Curator*

## § The Company of the Gate — northern Letia to Ulan-Tang, 3026 d.M.
*Frater Remigius Falx, Canon-Historian of the Holy Office, continuing.*

The Curator bids me name the companies of my own land. I could name a dozen the north
still sings; the Office would have me name one — the one Letia would unwrite, were
unwriting doctrine. It is the fattest file in this house, kept on the shelf we show and
copied, in part, onto the shelf we do not. The heathen chroniclers of the east gave the
affair a title — *The Gate to Unknown* — and I use theirs, the Office owning no title for
a thing it would rather had no name.

The charters attest this much. In the years about 3026, a company of the far north —
barbarians, a sorcerer, a hunter, a monk, and worse — was drawn east across the Erg to
Ulan-Tang, that heathen city where the ancient portal stands. There the fallen inquisitor
whose name is struck two entries above (XI) laboured to force the gate, being by then no
living man but a lich. The company's own sorcerer, a demonologist of Letia, carried the
**Key of Nothing** through the whole venture, and pacted, again and again, with Ahriman
and his demons — for a resurrection, for knowledge, for escape — until whatever he had
been was spent. Whether the company went to shut the gate
and failed, or went to serve it and succeeded, no charter I hold will say; report divides,
and the Office divides with it. This only is certain, and it is written into the world
where no clerk may strike it: the gate opened, the **Gaze-upon-Nothing** was drawn into
Ymir, and the red moon rose that owned no calendar. From that hour Theocrat, Technocrat,
and Great Khan alike read the sky and armed. The doctors call the age that followed the
Age of the Tide; my Register calls it the world's second wounding, and dates it here.
→ *canon/history.md*, 3026.

The company, as charter and report allow:

**Urichal, called Tofango** — of the northern clans, devout of Mithra before the axe took
his devotion from him. The greatest axeman of the age and its saddest man; his fuller file
stands above (XIII), and the Office does not try a man twice. Here it suffices that he bore
**Oblivion** into this venture, that the demon in the steel gnawed his governance away by
the road, and that he laid the axe down at the last and went up a remote mountain to end
his days a hermit upon it.

**Gundar, called the Butcher** — a brute of the same north and no pretence otherwise; the
company kept him for his fury and buried him for it. He fell in the deep places of the
venture and did not stay fallen: the demonologist bought him back from death by a pact with
Ahriman, and the thing that climbed out wearing Gundar carried a pestilence in it — the
small pox — which is the wage such bargains pay, and never to the buyer.

**Fiorentino Cipriano** — a merchant by his face and a demonologist beneath it; a secret
caster of the Void, a maker of golems, and keeper of the Key of Nothing from first to last.
He pacted with demons as other men change coats — for lives, for lore, for escape — and
each pact took its measure, until report has him standing at the full measure of
corruption: which is to say at the bottom of the ledger, with nothing left to pledge. The
Office has met the type. It has never met one who climbed back up.

**Sebastian Vemeer, called Nero** — a hunter of no great account, sold into slavery by the
company's own sorcerer once he had become a weight, and bought again by a trafficker who
dressed him in black and gave him back to the world as "Nero," the black hunter. Report
makes much of the reinvention; the Office makes little. A demon killed him. Demons commonly
do.

**Fèl Fenor** — an elf, and so beyond the Office's writ, out of the cursed kindred of the
Dream-world; report has him misread by his fellows, turned or driven to evil, and put down
at the last by the very companions who could not read him. An elf's tragedy, filed and not
judged, per the standing rule.

**War-Ra** — an assassin of the eastern house of **Ra**, princes of Ulan-Tang, who joined
the venture late and near its hinge. The heathen of that city breed killers as we breed
clerks. Mark the name, examiners: report east of the Erg gives it again, a generation on,
under the style *Secundus* — but that is another file, and it is not yet written.

**Kyrna Halish** — a hunter of the seven stones, who tracked the company through the waking
world and the Dream World alike for the keys they carried, and by whose pursuit the stones
changed hands more than once. Neither of the Faith nor against it; a professional.

Filed shorter: **Izàr the Silent**, a monk of Hudemia whose hand was chief among those that
brought down the paladin **Martius Magnus** aboard the Armorite flyer — a killing the Office
notes and declines to celebrate; **Tarek**, a cleric-sorcerer who turned his magic on his
own company in the thick of battle and vanished the instant it failed, betrayer confirmed
and never recovered; **Rekon**, a barbarian who joined at the end and took up Oblivion when
Tofango left it — read that clause twice, examiners; and **Arcade Nemo Manlio**, an
ex-Armorite of the diplomatic sort turned trafficker, who bought Vemeer out of the
slave-market and loosed the black hunter on the world.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* upon **Fiorentino Cipriano**, HERETIC and DEMONOLATER; DAMNED by
his own repeated hand, no coercion pleaded and none available. A man may be dragged to one
pact; he walks to the tenth. The file is closed as souls are closed — from the far side.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* upon **Gundar**, RAISED BY AN UNLAWFUL PACT; the resurrection
void in doctrine, and the pestilence its proof. What Ahriman returns, he returns freighted.
The Office need not have declined to recognise the second life; the pox unmade it inside the
year.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* upon **the Company entire**, INSTRUMENT — witting or unwitting
the Register cannot say, and has ruled that it cannot — OF THE WORLD'S SECOND WOUNDING.
Whether they meant to shut the gate or to force it, the gate is open; intent is Heaven's
province, and Heaven has the file. The Office records the hole in the sky and assigns the
hands nearest it.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* upon **War-Ra and the house of Ra**, HEATHEN, root and branch;
unbaptised, unexaminable, and — the name recurring — not done with us. Watch entered against
the house, and against the byname the east has not yet spent.

## § The Company of the Steamclad Sky — Armora & the Wald, the War of the Machines, from 3025 d.M.
*Named for the Companies of the Turning Age by **The Armora Crier**, which prints, as ever, all the news the steam can carry.*

Now here, Compilers, is a company the Crier will put up against any the southern chapters can field, and it cost this city nothing to raise but a war. You have read the Office's cold notices on our heroes a few leaves back; let the Crier warm them up. When the brothers of the Young Kingdoms fell to quarrelling — Armora, the Wald, and loyal Zaalesha on the one part; Vallanya, Caeldir, and Tydriell on the other — and a golden dragon out of the old dark, **Gwalgamaur**, invited itself and its orc-hordes to the estate, it was seven strangers, thrown together in a library break-in and a dockside brawl, who ran the whole rotten business to its root. What they turned up there the Palace still declines to print: that the war itself was *arranged* — a *casus belli* manufactured off two of our own mechanical warships, sold under the counter by a man named Keveth; a diabolical **Resurrection Engine** to drink the living and wake the dead; the **Black Sorcerers** in the shadows, the vampire lord **Leonard** behind them, and behind the whole of it the **Conspiracy of the Five**, five names this paper's founders knew better than to set in type. They gathered the six **Divine Relics** — Ignus, Saralis, Meletskar, Tassalung, Natharien, Oedring — carried the fight from our own sewers to the dragon's own fire-mountain, and came home victors. As for the vulgar rumour that in the doing they stripped certain sleeping **Runes of Power** from under this city and drowned Waldenheim, unbarred the Maelstrom, and loosed the **Bewitched Tide** upon a peaceful world — the Crier refers the reader to the Office of the Grand Duchess, whose position on buried runes is a matter of annual record: *mere superstition, unworthy of a modern people.* It makes, the Crier concedes, a magnificent story; we have sold a great many papers upon it.

### THE COMPANY, IN THEIR OWN PROPER PERSONS

**Isidoro VonGraven, called Tyrael** — Our own light-elementalist of the **Academy of Elemental Studies**, and the strangest civic ornament this city has yet produced: not born, the sober accounts insist, but *made* — a living likeness of the sorcerer **Sigurd VonGraven**, sharing that lord's very life-essence with a being of light named **Tyrael**, which now and again looks out of him. He keeps a cat, **Elerina**, and — the Crier prints this in the small hours and regrets it by morning — a private curiosity in the necromantic arts the Permit Board would not renew. (Register, entry XV; and mind the spelling — VonGraven, not the ducal VonGryffen.)

**Surya, called the Yellow Demon** — The easterner: an **Ur-Tanoth** swordsman who fled the far **Qi-Long** carrying the blade the east names the **Masamune**, which the College identifies with the Relic **Meletskar** — Dragon-God assassins baying at his heels the length of his flight. He cut down the dragon-lord **Vorak** and fell into a killing coma of it; and — this being an Armoran story — he got up again. (Register, entry XIV.)

**Drusilde** — An exiled **Xattva** archmage, once a teacher at our Academy, afterward foundress of her own celebrated school, the **Seventh Circle**; a scholar of such standing she is set down as author of a whole chapter of this very Compendium — the third, which the Crier's busy presses have not yet reached. Killed — the Crier reports it plainly — by a dwarf's own bomb bursting a hand's-breadth too near; and returned to the living, the accounts murmur, by a pact struck with an entity they take great care not to name.

> ✒ **Drusilde** · *undated* — The Crier says I came back "on a pact I will not name." I will not name it because I have not finished paying it, and a debt read aloud comes due faster. Ask me again in a chapter where I am still writing.

**Yonker, called Dwalor** — Our celebrated dwarf artificer, a genius of powder and the explosive charge, whose bronze war-walker stands garlanded in the Square of the Sciences — the beloved **YonkerMecha**. A dwarf-firster in his private heart, if the Ghetto's talk is to be trusted; hailed for his war-merits as heir of the Wald's own **Dwalor** clan, whose ring he took up over its murdered bearer and swore to avenge.

**Baudolino** — A **half-dwarf** marksman and mercenary of this city, the finest shot in the company and the least sentimental man in it, who came away from the war with a living black symbiote-weapon of no honest provenance grafted to his arm — a thing he has since, the surgeons say, learned to sleep beside.

**Han Hsian-Tz'** — A dancing-monk out of the far Qi-Long, of the secret order the east calls the **Grey Herons**, who crossed half of Ymir upon Surya's trail — not for the swordsman but for the sword, sworn to keep the Masamune from the hands of the Dragon-Gods. That two such men should finish as brothers-in-arms happens only in wars and in this newspaper.

**"Doc"** — A **Xidhe** man of science and a seer, cold as an essence-meter, given over to the *perfecting of his own race* by methods the Crier will style "advanced" and leave there.

## § The Cartosan Companies — the Principality, the Year of the Black Sun and after

*as set down by Bartolomé Aguirre de Garzona, called the Nightingale — bard of Cartosa, and author of that* True History of Roberto Rodrigo, the Armorite *which the good frater above, reading it, mistook for a book of beasts; who, given leave to name the companies of his own coast, names two, and loves them both.*

They gave the north its inquisitor and his dry little verdicts; then — Orum bless whatever clerk arranged it — they gave the south a bard. I am the bard, and I do not apologize for the rhymes: a copla holds a man longer than a charter, and kinder. The frater named my coast's great names and left out its great companies — and a company is the forge a name is beaten out on. Here are two of ours. Both put to sea after a treasure; both found a door instead. Very Cartosan.

### A Black Sun Rising — the Year of the Black Sun, 3017

Ask at any harbour for the Black Sun year and you get the tavern-song: the sun comes up *black as a hanged man's tongue*, and young Alberto Carrillo V — that men after called the Steady — holds the palace stair until the Navy brings the true dawn up from Santa Hermana. True, and thin as a purser's soup. Here is the thicker telling. What climbed out of the cellars with an eclipsed sun stitched on its breast was the Black Dawn, the cult of Ahriman and the Sovereign Demons; its smiling captain was Tobias, a tutor of the Blue Mantles no less, who read the Adepts' dead tongue off a scrap of gold and went hunting a buried king's sceptre in Cumbre de Oro, the golden city that is truly cursed Taremorn. Six strangers got in the cult's road — nobody's chosen champions, only what the tide leaves on the quay.

*The sun rose black o'er Cartosa town and the cellar-doors came wide; / six who owed the crown no love yet stood upon the crown's own side.*

- **Alfonso** — a Cartosan arcane elementalist of the Blue Mantles, and the one sober head among them. When the warrant grew too heavy to carry a registered mage, he burned his blue cloak, renounced the Order, and retired to Ombra del Mar, the pirates' free-port — the wisest man in my tale.
- **Mjolnirr, called Don Pablo** — a Cartosan dwarf up out of the criminal quarters who, between one gutter and the next, caught the mad faith of Heis, god of Chance and Wind. He built himself a flying machine of scrap and conviction, went up in it, and has not come down. *Don Pablo trusted wind and wing, and Heis, who is not kind; / the wind gave back the wing to us. Don Pablo stayed behind.*
- **Raphael** — a Cartosan dwarf, swordsman and loud champion of dwarf-rights in a city that keeps its own for a ghetto. In the golden town he gave his sword-hand to the ghost of King Lorith to buy the others their door, and gave the rest of himself to the Black Dawn, fighting; the ghetto sings him yet.
- **Ruben Riccardo Montoya, "Death-in-Life"** — a Xion captain-cleric of Heis, scarred crown to heel, a lover of the dead and a dreamer of impossible engines, who meant to raise a temple of Chaos that would fly. When the cult came down he was found among neither the living nor the dead.
- **Valerio Luis y Albericio** — a half-elf mystic in the loosest sense: a charlatan of real gifts and a con-man of greater ones, with a price on his head in three ports and a smile in all of them. He talked the company out of a fortress the once it needed it, and fled at last to the Silver Isles.
- **Kiki** — a Tal-Tanoth kitten of the Old City, all whisker and quiet larceny, who ran with the company one caper and no longer. She came unasked; then, catlike, the chair was warm and Kiki gone.

### The Monster Mash — the hunt for the Armorite's hoard

Roberto Rodrigo, called the Armorite for the northern land that bred him, spent more gold in his life than ever crossed the Prince's counting-house, and hid the last and largest hoard of it where only he could go, drawing a map and carrying the reading of it down to the gallows of Fort Leon. His crew came late; a storm held them; he cursed them, dying; and they were gone, ship and souls, into weather that did not give them back.

*They hanged the Armorite at Fort Leon when the storm would not come in; / and his crew sails yet to fetch him home, on the far side of the wind.*

Long years after, three who should never have shared a deck came by the map, and by the route beneath it, and by the curse beneath the route — that last unread. They sailed regardless, and are the reason my *True History* runs to two volumes and reads, the frater swears, like a bestiary.

- **Raggot-Tod** — a half-Tanoth smuggler of no fixed harbour, said (by the men who will not sail under him) to captain the cursed hull between the waking sea and the sea of nightmares, and to bring her home with her hold full and her crew not quite the tally that put out.
- **Gloria Duarte** — a Cartosan inquisitor of Hudemia, the Just Mother of Waters: severe, unbribable, and — here is the part I love — shipped aboard a contrabandist's deck of her own grim will. An inquisitor at sea with smugglers is the whole south in one sentence, and I have set it to music.
- **Diego Alehandro de la Corona** — a troubadour of noble blood and dissolute habit, famed coast to coast for ballads black enough to curdle milk: the better-born singer and the worse poet. That he shipped on that voyage and I did not is the one debt the Armorite's ghost still owes me. My rival, my friend, and wrong always about meter.

### The Verdicts of the Office, reproduced

The frater has filed my rogues as diligently as he filed my Rodrigo, and I set his rulings down as I found them. These are weighings of piety on a Solanthyan scale; a southern man reads them as he reads a customs-declaration out of Meridian, and discounts the ink eight in the hundred.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMERS, both, of a god the register declines to dignify with a capital — the airy idol **Heis**, whom the south names the Mad and the Office names nothing; frenzy being not devotion, and a temple that flies being not a temple. UNREPENTANT, on the grounds of being unfindable.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* SCHISMATIC — the woman **Duarte**, an inquisitrix of the water-idol Hudemia: a heretic set to examine heretics, a chair the Holy Office does not concede exists. Zealous by report, misdirected by definition. Filed in the drawer kept for rivals it must on no account admire.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* LAX — **Valerio**, a mystic by trade and a fraud by vocation, with a price on him in three ports and a creed in none. FUGITIVE. A soul so practised at vanishing has spared the Office the labour of a sentence; entered under *fled*.

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* LAX BEYOND THE REACH OF EXAMPLE — the whole southern company, root and branch, after the very manner of the pirate **Roberto Rodrigo** at whose hoard they grasped: no mass among them, no tithe, no oath the sea did not dissolve. Condemned in absentia, the absentia being, as ever on these coasts, precisely the point.

The frater ends satisfied — but no Cartosan leaves the last word to a Letian. He calls me, in his register, the Armorite's *second biographer*, and wonders in cold ink whether my subject can be baptized at all. He cannot; the frater knows the reason and will not print it, and neither will I. I only sing it:

*He kept no mass, he paid no tithe, he broke his oath at will — / save one, sworn once across the cards; and he is keeping it still.*

## § The Gaerwath Companies — the Cabal, and the Fever-Breakers

### The Purple Cabal
*A company of the Gaerwath demon-realm, as set down by Aelryn Vhaeloth i-Celeblyar, Loremaster of the Drowned Athenaeum.*

You asked me, Curator, for a company of my own country, and I give you one that shows my
country small — the only way an elf is ever ashamed, and so the most instructive to the brief.
Three Hyrul who had passed the trial and not yet made themselves anyone; children of good Names,
with silver enough to buy what talent had not yet earned; and they styled themselves, in the
fashion of the young who have read much and bled little, the **Purple Cabal** — purple being the
colour a lesser Name puts on when it wishes to be mistaken for the Shining one. The affair that bound them was squalid in the ordinary way. A sealed
letter and a coffer passed between the factions by two apprentice couriers; a Mechanist master
found at his desk with the life drawn out of him and his skin gone to parchment; a Demonologist
of standing *butchered* — with a blade, in a Necromancer's lodging, which among us is not murder
but vulgarity, we keeping quieter arts for that. The coffer held a great book in the tongue
the Forces speak, which none of them could read and all of them understood: not a prize, a leash,
and the hand that had plaited it still upcountry, unhurried. They killed a watcher, unmade the
body in the tidy Gaerwath way, and grasped at last that they were not the players but the pieces.
Mark the sequel, for it is my whole point. In your world three corpses and a stolen grimoire end
a career. In mine they begin one. By this present hour — the nine hundred and sixty-eighth year
of the Eternal's reign — the survivors sit robed in the learned colleges, consulted, and quite
untroubled.

**Rag'shagan** — a Necromancer now, a Biomancer once. In the body-change that makes our children
adult he woke in flesh a rival school had grown in a poisoned vat, to avenge a grief a century
stale; his own House put him out, the Necromancers took him in, as they take in all the ruined.
He wears the flayed face of the body he was born to nailed over the deformed one he was given,
and finds it, I am told, an improvement. He sits today among the **True Seers**.

**Ychan, called the Weak** — scion of one of the first Demonologist Houses, so named for a body

## § The Naming of the Frozen Isle
### *Helgedad, the war-winters, ~3015–3024 d.M.*
**Being a naming-roll — a *drápa* — of the frozen isle, as sung by Hrafnkel Ognirsson, skald of the Nortmaar, before the war-hearths; caught by the clerks, the skald still refusing the pen.**

Fill the horn again, southlander. In the great telling I gave you the isle; here I give you the folk the war-winters made and unmade — the standers, the fallers, the turned. A saga warms a hall. A roll does not. A roll only keeps a man, the way the ice keeps him: whole, and cold, and past all asking.

> Hear the harp-count, hall-sitters:
> the wolf-winters' wound-rich roll.
> Thurms of the thunder was throned in the north,
> Heis in his madness, grim Gog under —
> three hard crowns for a killing age,
> and frost the father of all the fallen.

In the year the southrons write **3015** came the **Winter of Unrest**, and the wolves that were no wolves: a host herald-less and hunger-bannered, five faceless captains whose likeness no living captive ever gave. In the **Siege of One Day** they supped in Geidmund's high seat at dusk and were smoke by dawn, and Nortislong, our eldest city, was theirs for a single sun. Then **Berthrant Vaelaer** — the southron keeps his own leaf on him, the eighth — would not let the leavings live, and hunted the wolf-scraps five winters through, **3015 to 3020**; his muster is the great lay, and I'll not tax the horn twice. One winter we breathed. Then in **3021** they came again in full pelt, and no single day carried it: **three years** the **War of the Wolves** ground the marches to meal, and by **3024** we stood victors over a foe whose face we never saw. Thurms took his tithe of the brave, Heis diced for the rest, grim Gog gathered what neither would own. Here are four the three gods marked.

**Dwayne Soylen, the Builder** — a paladin of Thurms, thunder's sworn, who raised temples where other war-men raise camps: wherever he wintered, a roof went up to the storm-father, and the roofs — curse the man — held. The south has since hallowed him **Saint Dwayne** by their own acclaim, with a written *Life* and shrines, and roof-beams brought to be blessed (their clerk files him at his nineteenth leaf, and grinds a tooth to do it). A heathen names a foreign folk's saint the way he names foreign weather: real enough where it falls on other men, and no god of ours.

> ✒ **a sapper of the Kelmark garrison** · *3524* — They tell us there is no Saint Dwayne, that Solanthya struck him three times over. We set the footings by his name regardless, for the man's roofs held and ours hold, and a soldier prays to whatever kept the last roof up over the last man who slept dry under it. Strike him a fourth time, reverend fathers. The beams do not hear you.

**Kamal, called the Mad** — high priest of mad Heis on the isle, and once, if you credit it, a dwarf: a stone-blooded delver no taller than your two-hand sword. He swallowed a seed of the **Tree of Chaos**, and the seed kept his god's faith with him — it swelled him past all shape into a thing deformed and holy, a fane of flesh his own priests knelt to and dreaded. Heis loves no jest so well as the one that eats the jester. Kamal is his fondest.

**Kronen Vorab** — a paladin of grim Gog, the self-coiled Serpent, god of the Damned and of Lies and Thurms's ancient enemy; and I give him honest, for a skald who prettifies is a liar with a tune. Two things made his name, neither kind: a cruelty that wanted no cause, and a tongue so sweet it could coax a widow out of her grief and her grain in one breath. Men followed the honey down onto the knife. Gog is patron of the faceless, and the Wolves' five wore no faces; make of that a shiver and no more.

**Varg** — a Vorskgen of the frozen wastes, my own blood's blood, a swordsman who came to love the sword-craft better than the warm hand that held it. To make his war perfect he poured his soul out of its living cage into a body of cold metal, that no fear might shake his edge and no weariness dull it. And he perfected it. His stroke wants nothing now — and neither does he, for want is a warm thing, and he left his in the flesh he shed. He stands in the ice yet, they tell, flawless, and empty as a struck bell.

Now the verdicts. The clerk at Solanthya keeps a book of us as we keep a book of our dead, save that his is bound in disapproval; I read it back that you may laugh where I laughed. First, our new-minted saint:

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* SAINT BY ACCLAMATION, IN A COMMUNION NOT OURS; which is to say, NOT A SAINT — three times ruled, three times re-shrined; suppression pending, and the funds for it pending longer.

A saint, says the south, is a thing their office stamps. We asked for no stamp. Thurms keeps no registry; Dwayne's roofs hold without a seal from Solanthya, which is one miracle more than the clerk will sign.

Then on the priests of grim Gog and mad Heis:

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMERS; the northern creeds barbarous, their gods false, their rites anathema, pronounced.

Blasphemers against what, I asked — against the warm god, said the reader. Against ours they are only devout. Ours are not warm, southlander: Heis is madness and Gog is the Serpent, and we named them true and knelt anyway. That is not blasphemy but honesty, which his office files under sin.

Last, on the isle entire:

⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* SCHISMATIC BY GEOGRAPHY; the island seceded, and its creeds are Thurms's and colder; entered whole among the strayed.

We walked out from under the eagle-standards in the year they write **2600**, and took our gods with us as a man takes his own axe out of a bad hall. Strayed, he writes — from his road, aye, and we were never once upon it. (See his eighth leaf and his nineteenth; the clerk and I agree on every fact and on nothing beyond it.)

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The Tree of Chaos whose seed unmade Kamal is no turn of phrase. It is the **Heart of Chaos**
> that took root at the centre of the wood thereafter called the **Tangle** in 1600 d.M. — raw,
> world-warping Essence that seeds and buds. One seed carried north and swallowed did to a single
> dwarf what the Heart does to a whole forest: it did not kill him, it **re-authored** him. What
> kneels in the isle's Heis-shrines is a bud of the Tangle wearing a priest's memory. What a grove
> of Kamals would be is a door the table may open.*

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *Varg's soul poured into forged metal is not the first such working, only the crudest and saddest
> — a poor man's echo of the **Adepts' made-things**: the bound-spirit golems the Dark Elves still

> themselves, wills forged into elemental bodies as living weapons. **Nihoggr** sleeps beneath the
> isle's own ice by that same craft (Ch. VIII). Deeper still runs the **labyrinth of the Lord of
> Oblivion** in the Aestyr's roots, a prison of forgetting that unmakes a will as surely as Varg
> unmade his own. The frozen isle is full of empty perfect things; Varg is the smallest, and the only one that chose it.*

## § A Gallery of Wanderers
### *the renowned who signed no company's book — as told by Pips Quondam, Tal-Tanoth, who has travelled with more of them than she would say in a court*

The Curator asked me for the ones who never joined anybody: the freelances, the loners, the sort who turn up when the work is interesting and are three provinces away when the bill comes. I know most of them. I have shared a fire or a cell with the better half. Here they are, charm and warts alike, and the cat will tell you which is which.

**Asternak-Gaib** — of the great House Gaib at Ulan-Tang, a mage who took to the god Yogh the way other men take to drink, and with the same effect on the neighbours: a depraved and clever hand, and I have locked more doors against him than against any man living. Brother to Yirk.

**Yirk-Gaib** — the quiet brother, which in that family is the frightening one. A rogue with hands as good as mine and a silence I never once got under, and — the temple swears it, and he lets them — a chosen of Yogh himself. When both Gaibs are in a room, count the exits, then count the brothers.

> ⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* BLASPHEMERS, the pair; U.-T. cult of Yogh; files kept joined, on the reasoning that they will die joined.

**Christabella** — a Fae seer no higher than my knee and twice as far away, who walks the Dreamlands the way I walk a market-street: knows the stalls, knows the cutpurses, comes home with more than she took. If she tells you your dream had a door in it, pay her and go round.

**Gregorio VanErsputin** — an Armoran holy-man, and here is my whole theology of holy-men in one line: the charm is real and the halo is rented. Gregorio has enough of the first to sell you a great deal of the second. I have watched a room go soft for him, and I have counted my rings after.

> ⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* FRAUD; or, the margin of the file adds, in a later hand, WORSE.

**Lita** — a witch out of the frozen north, sly and sweet-spoken and about as warm as the country that raised her; the kind who does you a small kindness so you will owe her a large one. I took a favour from Lita exactly once. I am, technically, still paying.

> ⟢ *Verdict of the Office:* TO BE EXAMINED — the Office's politest sentence, and its most ominous.

**Ming-Chia** — a monk out of the Qi-Long who mislaid his mind somewhere on the eastern road and seems to have found something better in the empty space. Talks to weather, wins fights he has no business surviving, and once explained the whole of existence to me while hanging upside-down in a plum tree. I understood the plum tree.

**Salaphard Panempiricous** — an archmage and philosopher of formidable equipment and one small habit: he says he was born on a planet named Tchyo. I have crossed most of the map and never found the road to it — but then I never found the road to half of what turned out to be true, so the cat reserves judgement.

**Samael** — a half-elf born to a good name in the Young Kingdoms, who kept the worst company on Ymir, the Sovereign Demons themselves, until he traded up — if you call it up — to a pact with the dragon Gildiss. A man who changes masters that high is not climbing; he is being carried. Delightful at dinner. Do not be his creditor.

**Syena** — an Ur-Tanoth of the Sierra Plata, one of my great gentle cousins, and if you have read my chapter you know I do not joke about those. A barbarian bound so close to the Spirits she half-lives among them; the towns frighten her, and she is right. I would open any cage in the world to keep that one out of one.

**Tara** — a princess of Ophiur with a throne taken out from under her, who went and learned dark magic the way you would learn a trade, for the plainest and worst of reasons: to pay somebody back. At last report she is missing — which in her field of study is either the beginning of the answer or the end of the question.

**Gurthak** — an orc off the mountains of Alturia, barbarous as advertised, and one of perhaps three of his kind I would sit downwind of by choice. Keeps his word, which is more than Alturia taught him and more than several dukes ever learned.

**Ferdinando Montecarlos** — a pathfinder of Cartosa, born and raised, who hires out to go where the maps stop and — this is the whole trick of him — comes back to complain about it. Every guild in the south claims to have trained him; he trained himself, on other people's coin, a career I respect professionally.

## § The Register of the Fallen
### *the Curator keeps the roll the companies could not*

Frater Falx entered the safely dead. I must enter the others — those who fell in the turning of the age and left no shrine behind them, only a gap in a company where a name had been. I take them in the order the one surviving ledger gives, which begins, as such ledgers should, with the name the survivors could not stop saying.

**Kharim Abdul Jabbar** stands first: a Common-Man thief of the Great Erg, who spoke the desert tongue, wore a ring of *Unseen* upon a hand no one thought to watch, and kept at his heel a bound storm-hound — a Zephyr Hound — that went before him into every room he did not trust. His is the first name on the fallen-roll, and the roll is the truer for opening where it does.

After him, in the quiet a register keeps for such things: where now is **Raphael**, who fell to the Black Dawn in the year the sun went dark over Cartosa? Where **Sebastian Vemeer**, whom his company called **Nero**, taken by a demon at a gate he should never have stood before? Where **Fèl Fenor**, put down at the last by his own people — because they loved the world more than they could keep loving him, and he had left them no third door? Where **Mjolnirr**, whom the south still toasts as **Don Pablo**, lost to the very sky he had set his heart on flying? They are where the age put them, which is beneath it.

And after these, the ones no register names at all: the sword-hands and the lantern-bearers, the ones who died on the first page of a tale another finished — of whom a company keeps a face, a debt, and a way of laughing, and no clerk on Ymir wrote a word. The Office measures piety; the cat measures charm; this page measures only the distance they are gone. *Requiescant.* The book continues because they did not.

## § The War That Has Not Happened ⟡ **GM ONLY** ⟡

> ⟡ **GM ONLY. What follows belongs to the one who runs the world. If the last two pages read as honest, stop here — this one is not honest, and may not be true for anyone.** ⟡

You have read the frater's eleventh entry, and my note beneath it. Here is the rest of the note, because a name like **M'Raker** is not left half-copied.

On the lowest shelf — locked, the frater said, not empty — lie pages no synod commissioned and no synod could bring itself to burn. They describe a war the world has not yet had, and they give it in the past tense, which is the first thing wrong with them.

In them, the struck inquisitor finishes what he began at Ulan-Tang. The lich takes the name M'Raker and stops hiding. He raises the dead of every field the age has filled — and the age has filled a great many — and drives them across the world under no banner but their own rot. **Armora is besieged, and Armora falls**: the steam-guns, the schismatic Church, the whole proud Duchy of engines, cracked like a dropped watch. The gods are barred from Ymir's sky — the pantheon that is half power and half costume shut out together, the true powers and the thieves latched on the far side of one door, and the world left beneath an empty heaven with the lich for its only weather. And M'Raker is nowhere a sword can reach him. He has carried his phylactery into the far south, past the Ivory coast into the deep Ophiur, and buried the one thing that ends him where no army will think to march. There he waits for what the pages call the restoration — the hour the gods are let back into their sky — because he means to come home through the same door they do, wearing a borrowed divinity, entered on the rolls of heaven as a demigod before anyone thinks to count the newcomers twice.

That is the war on the lowest shelf. Now the only honest line in it: **no other record says a word of it is so.** The pages bear no seal, no hand I can place, no date I will trust. They are a true prophecy set down too early, or a forgery salted into the crypt to arm someone or frighten them, or a nightmare that happened to keep very good handwriting — and nothing decides which. At ~3518 the world has had no such war. Kramer's workings sit around their gate, patient; his phylactery has never been found. That is the whole of what the true records grant, and not one grain more.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *M'Raker is Kramer, arrived: dead armies, Armora fallen, the sky shut on gods and thieves alike, the phylactery in the deep Ophiur, the lich waiting to re-enter heaven behind a god's face. Proscribed, undated, vouched by nothing — future-arc, forgery, or dream. If you open that door at your table, you are choosing the age's worst ending out of a drawer that offered you the choice. Choose it knowing what you arm.*
