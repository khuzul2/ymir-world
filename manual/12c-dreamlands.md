# XII·C · THE BOOK OF THE SLEEPING SHORE
## *the Dreamlands, and Kyria under its veil, set down by one who piloted sleepers for hire*

**From the retirement memoir of Hesper Vaneck of Armora, thirty years dream-pilot of the licensed house at the sign of the Bell & Candle, Glassharp Lane, the New City; styled on her licence — No. 114 in the registry of the College of the Seventh Circle — Conductor of Assisted Sleep, second grade. Deposited at the Crystal Seat, unasked, beneath fifty-one volumes of descent-logs.**

> *Curator's note.* This leaf was not commissioned. It arrived two winters after the book was
> sewn, in a deal crate under the log-volumes of a licensed Armorite dream-house; the arithmetic
> of their waking-times reconciles to the minute across thirty years, which nothing else in this
> Compendium can claim. The covering letter asked only that the logs be kept somewhere that does
> not burn. The memoir lay underneath them, unmentioned. I bind it among the late leaves because
> the desert chapter ends standing at a door into this same country, and because the late leaves
> are where this book keeps the accounts that came in after the accounts were closed. The reader
> will notice the narrator's tenses misbehave. I queried one passage and received her only
> reply: *"It is not a slip. It is the tense the country keeps."* — *the Curator*

---

I was thirty years on the night-lists of the **Bell & Candle**, a licensed dream-parlour of the
New City, and I made four thousand nine hundred and six logged descents into the country this
leaf concerns. The trade calls me a **dream-pilot**. My licence — I have it before me; it wants
dusting — calls me a **Conductor of Assisted Sleep, second grade**, which is what the Permit
Office believes a dream is: a conveyance respectable persons are assisted into and out of, like
a tram. Both names are honest. Neither is sufficient. I will keep to the tram-language as long
as I am able; it is the language of my log-books, and my log-books are the only part of me that
has never lied.

Understand one thing and the rest will sit straight. The Compendium's other chapters describe
countries you may decline to visit. Not this one. **You go every night.** Every sleeper on Ymir
— the Theocrat on his cushions, you, reader, some hours from now — goes down into the **Dream
World** as water goes downhill, and comes back at dawn with wet hair and no memory. The learned
call the country a province of the Spirit World, folded against ours like silk against a lining;
I have felt the fold. Passage, then, is free, universal, and wasted on everyone. What a licensed
parlour sells is **arrival**: going down awake behind your own eyes, with a pilot who knows the
way home and a bell already set for your return.

Weigh the rest of my testimony by what a sitting was like. The client takes the couch; I take
the chair; between us the **Oneiroscope**, warm, smelling faintly of rain on brass; at the wall
the house wizard watches the Essence-meter. I enter the waking-time in the log — the waking-time
*first*, before the name, before the tariff; a pilot who enters the fee before the bell is a
pilot I will not sit with. The lamp is lowered. The bell above the couch is struck once, softly,
so the sleeping ear will know its voice at the far end. And then — forgive me; this is the tense
the country keeps — *then we are on the Stair; the mind will not go down without a metaphor to
hold. Below, the water of sleep lies black and warm, lit from underneath, and the first shore
shows itself the way it always shows: a lamp you remember from childhood, in a window you cannot
possibly remember. We step off. The sand takes the weight of two. I count my fingers — the
oldest exercise of the trade — and there are five, and we have arrived, and the whole enormous,
kindly treachery of the place opens before my client like a garden.*

Then the bell, and tea. Ninety marks the sitting, waking-times observed to the minute. The
licence is not a tax but a tether: the Crier counted eleven tourists lost last season to houses
promising more than the tariff-board, and all eleven sat at unlicensed couches. In thirty years
the Bell & Candle lost nobody. Nobody, I should write, but one; and I will come to her.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The pilot believes the fee ends at the tariff-board. It does not. The Dreamlands are a
> province, and provinces have rulers: the **Outer Gods** hold this one. Sealed out of waking
> creation, the Ancient Gods were never sealed out of sleep — the one border the Adepts could
> not close — and they keep it as landlords, patient as tide: every night the whole world
> crosses into their reach and comes back unremembering, and the parlours' little bells ring
> because something tolerates the ringing. And the road has a keeper: **Topramesk the Yellow**,
> last of the seven who broke the world, holds the dream-gate of drowned Ragash and polices
> every lucid crossing out of Ymir — for the dreamers' safety, the records say, without saying
> against what. No one has asked what the true toll is, or to whom the door is paid.*

**Now the geography, as far as a second-grade licence runs.** The near country the trade calls
**the Hem** — the shallows just under the surface of sleep, where everything is sewn out of the
sleeper. The Crier's advertisement is for once exact: gardens no gardener planted, cities of
brass, skies you will not describe at breakfast. The Hem is safe the way a mirror is safe:
nothing in it but what you brought. Below it lie **the Meadows**, and there the trade begins in
earnest, for the Meadows are *shared*. You know a thing is not yours by the way it fails to need
you — an inn that goes on standing when no one dreams in it. A pilot learns the fixed
**landfalls** as a coastwise master learns his marks — not by star or needle, for there are
none, but by *conviction*: you steer by what you can hold yourself certain of, which is why the
trade takes calm persons and ruins vain ones. Consider **Dream Steel**, the metal mined, they
say, under that country: I have held a knife of it, and it weighed exactly what I believed it
weighed.

Below the Meadows — in the plainest tram-language I own — lie **the deeper shores**, and the
deeper shores are not priced in marks. Nothing down there wants your gold. The far country
trades in waking things, and names its own price: hours first, for time there runs as it pleases
— a forty-minute sitting can cost a day and a half; then the fine grain of memory, so that a man
comes home whole but can no longer say his mother's smell; then names. A colleague of mine paid
a name once. He is well. He is *called* something now, the way a shop is called something. That
is the tariff of the deep, and no bell rings there softly.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *Past the deeper shores, in order: **Ragash-below** — the drowned capital's shadow, standing
> whole in the dream with every window lit, the fixed point through which every lucid crossing
> out of Ymir must pass, which is why the gatekeeper keeps it. Beyond Ragash, the **Desert of
> Lost Dreams**, which everyone who has tried it calls infinite. It is not. On its far side
> hangs **Arcadia**: a steam-wreathed world of islands suspended in an ocean of air, where the
> Essence blows in Winds and is bottled in **Van Argan Stones** — batteries of refined Banite
> that burn for ever, the engine-heart of a civilisation of gliders and rotor-ships. Its great
> power is the **Crystal Empire** of the **Kraeva** — grey-skinned, bloodlessly courteous,
> certain they are Arcadia's only rightful people — prosecuting an armed peace under a
> **Prophecy of the Return**; their captives "wear the White" and serve unarmed. No dreamer of
> Ymir has crossed the Desert; almost none suspects a far side. **Topramesk alone certainly
> knows Arcadia exists** — and a Return, in a world reachable only through his gate, would have
> to come out through something. Ask what.*

**The parlour is the paying door, but it is not the only door.** In the south they do without
engines: the **Green Lotus** of the Great Erg opens the mind in a two-hour coma, and every dose
gambles the soul slipping loose into the Dream World with no bell set — the flower does for a
copper what my house did for ninety marks, the way a flood does the work of a ferry. In the deep
Erg the nomads rope their camels away from **dream-gates** that do not open onto the desert, and
a seer of Ulan-Tang sells the crossing to those who must; somewhere past those gates, in a
prison built as a palace, the archmage **Mubafer** keeps the sweetest parlour of all — the
pilots' rule for him is one sentence: drink nothing, envy nothing, and do not let him show you
the library. And some need no door. Some are born arriving — awake behind their own eyes on the
first shore as children; the trade calls them **Dreamers**, half in envy. A few learn to walk
out of their own sleep into the common deep and home again, carrying — knowledge, mostly; some
swear, small things that fit in a closed hand — and these are the **Travellers**, for whom the
Permit Office's alphabet keeps no page, which tells you its whole opinion of them.

**From the log of Descent 3,441, ninth of Serpent, 3502; client a College man, Permit C,
question not stated; down at the third bell of night, waking-bell the sixth.** *We go deep. Past
the last landfall I hold certain — the unlit Lighthouse, which every pilot knows and none has
climbed — the water of sleep changes colour, and there is the garden. Hedges of black glass; no
wind. At the centre a woman made of brass and porcelain sits with her own winding-key in her
lap. She is weeping. The tears are crystal, and fall with the sound of very small bells, and the
gravel is white with them — drifts of them, years of them. She says, to no one, in the voice of
a public clock: "I am Vespertina. It is not yet paid." My client opens his mouth to ask what is
not paid. I strike the bell myself.* — In that log's incident-column my own hand has written:
*none*. That was the one cowardice of my licensed life, and I mend it here, in a book instead.
Every deep pilot knows the garden; no licensed log carries it. And the name is the name on the
Peacemaker's monuments — a Grand Duchess four centuries and a half dead, who ended the Great War
and gave this calm age its calm. What her name is doing on the lips of a weeping engine at the
bottom of sleep I do not know; I know only that the men who might know have made very sure it is
nobody's trade to ask.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The garden is real, and the log's "none" was the lie. **Vespertina VonGryffen**, the
> Peacemaker, ended the Great War and reconciled Armora with Letia — by statecraft, say the
> histories. Say instead: by contract. She went down, by the College's first engine, to treat
> with the far country's landlords for the calm of the world above; and the far shores take only
> the coin the pilot names — waking things. Her line got the age its peace. The dream got her:
> kept, wound, running down against a term. The country remembers her as a machine because that
> is what the bargain made of her — an engine of the calm, ticking. She weeps because she has
> read the lease; "it is not yet paid" is not a complaint but a countdown. The Veiled Hand
> calls this age's calm patience, and the patience not ours; ask whether the two are one
> instrument. What she bought, and in which year it falls due, are left open on purpose: a calm
> with a due-date is the best clock a campaign can keep.*

**Last, the island — and the island is why any of this is written.** Sailors know **Kyria** as
fifty leagues of fog that a full gale will not move; they sound it, find no gap, and stand away.
Under the fog, the traders say, lies an island the old ruinations never touched, and one town,
**Thonis**, where beast-faced folk — the shy Ur-Tanoth — keep lamps lit for nobody; and Thonis,
though no chart says so, is the one **waking anchorage** the dream-country has: the single town
on Ymir that stands in both weathers at once, ashore in the world and moored in the dream. I
know both reports are right. I have been there. Once. Descent 1,119, twelfth of Unicorn, 3488, a
calibration sitting, the house's own, no client aboard; and thirty years have not moved one
grain of it.

*The mist opens the way a hand opens. There is a town. Lamps in the mist, hung too kindly to be
harbour-lights; streets of turf; a smell of wet grass and clean fur. The folk come out
unhurried, cat-faced, grave, kind as aunts, and nobody asks my name, and I understand — the
understanding of dreams, that arrives without a doorway — that I am being read: turned over
gently, page by page, as a border-clerk turns papers, by the mist itself, which is in some
fashion also the town. They give me tea. I sit among them the length of a childhood. At last the
eldest of them — white-furred, with eyes the colour of the hour before dawn — takes my cup as
though I had finished, and says, kindly, in my own mother's voice, the sentence I have spent
thirty years contradicting: "Not you; not yet." And the mist folds me out of the town the way a
mother folds a child's arms into a coat — and I am on my own couch at the Bell & Candle, weeping
into the smell of rain on brass, and the waking-bell has not yet been struck.*

I logged it — *landfall; inhabited; turned back* — and the College lifted the page within the
month. No matter. I have it by heart, which is where they cannot file it.

> *Curator's note.* Compare Chapter V: three of the navigator's crew, asleep off this same fog,
> dreamed one town — lamps, mist, folk "kind as aunts" — in the very words this pilot chooses

Thirty licensed years I steered for that fog — every deep sitting trimmed a little
north-by-sleep, always with the bell set, always home to the minute — and I never raised it
again. But mark what an old pilot marks. Thonis shelters refugees; on this every trader agrees.
And no ship has *ever* landed there. By what road does a refugee reach an island the sea is not
permitted to touch? There is one road only that does not mind water. **They arrived asleep.**
Which means the island keeps some. Which means there is a list, and a door, and a doorkeeper;
and I have met her; and she said *not yet*.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *All true, and truer than she dares. The fog is a veil of the Dreamlands, drawn over Kyria
> seventeen centuries into the age to hide the island from the **Sovereign Demons**: Kyria was
> folded halfway into the Dream World and has been kept there since — untouched, unaging, time
> within passing as the dream pleases. What the veil guards is the **Dreaming Legion**: a secret
> order of men, Tanoth, cloud-giants, and stranger volunteers, drilling for centuries toward the
> last battle against the Sovereigns, under **Gildiss** — a Dream Dragon, a follower of
> Topramesk, who can lift the shadow of Corrupted Essence out of a living soul. The veil reads
> every sleeper it touches because it is recruiting: the "refugees" of Thonis arrived exactly as
> the pilot reasons — asleep, chosen, kept. Wardens do not refuse; they file. "Not you; not
> yet" is a posting-order with the date left blank, because the last battle has not yet called
> for pilots. When it calls, the Legion will want someone who can bring a thousand sleepers down
> in good order and hold a bell so it does not ring. The fog knows where she lives. So, now, do
> you.*

So I will confess the descent that is in none of the fifty-one volumes, since the Permit Office
has already been paid its opinion of it. In my thirty-first year, on the fourth of Magpie, 3511,
I let myself into my own house at the second bell of night, alone — no second pilot, no house
wizard, no client, and (write it plainly, Vaneck) **no waking-time**. I set no bell. A bell
calls you home, and I did not want to be called home. I went down past my grade and past my
craft, to where the mist begins; and the mist read me again, gently, to the last page; and it
sent me back *anyway* — saying, this time, nothing at all, which is worse. The house found me on
the third day; time had run as it pleased. The College took my licence, and the Permit Office
takes eleven marks a month for the rest of my life, which is the smaller debt. Since that night
I do not dream. My sleep is one grey water with no shore in it, and somewhere off in the grey a
lamp that does not come nearer. I am paying, you see, in the far country's own coin — waking
things — and I keep the instalments punctually, every night. I was always punctual.

I am old now; I sleep more than I wake; and the trade taught me exactly one prayer, which is a
waking-time. So let this leaf stand as my petition, filed where the fog can read it — for it
reads; I have felt the thumb at the corner of the page. To whom it concerns, in Thonis or above
Thonis: the references are enclosed, fifty-one volumes of them. Thirty years; every sleeper
brought home to the minute but one, and the one was myself, and on that account I have paid
nightly ever since. I am quiet. I am punctual. I know how to sit beside the sleeping, and how to
hold a bell so it does not ring. And if the answer is still *not yet*, then file me — and
*there: the lamp is in the window. The stair is under my foot. The mist is opening the way a
hand opens. I am not asleep, reader; I am early. Hold the bell.*

> *Curator's note.* The crate bore no address; the carter's bill named a fishing village on the

> neighbour's hand: the pilot sleeps longer each month, wakes cheerful, and asks that her
> waking-time be left unset. I have entered the leaf accordingly. — *the Curator*
