# XIII · THE PEOPLES OF YMIR
## *Whom You Will Meet on the Road, and How Not to Be Killed by Them*

**As taken down from the table-talk of Pips Quondam, Tal-Tanoth of no fixed address — traveller, guide, and dealer in goods of variable legality; by her own styling, "One Who Has Borrowed From Most of Them."**

> *Curator's note.* Scholars have written on the peoples of Ymir. I did not want a scholar. I
> wanted somebody who has slept in a Vorskgen longhouse, haggled with a Sohleugir through a veil,
> and been thrown out of Ost-en-Galad at least once — and who would talk if fed. Mistress Quondam
> dictated this over nine suppers at Elendor, each supper larger than she is, and I have checked
> her facts, which hold far more often than her manner suggests. Her slanders are her own. As for
> her trade: she says "recovery of mislaid property," and I have counted the spoons. — *the Curator*

---

You want to know about the peoples of the world, and you have asked a cat who has robbed— *travelled with* most of them, so you have asked correctly. I will tell you what they look like, what they eat, what insults them, and what they will pay for, because that is what keeps you alive on a road; theology keeps you alive nowhere. We are deep in the long calm, the Nuovo Equilibrio, and the roads are as safe as they have ever been. Which is not very. Attend.

**Humans first, because you cannot avoid them.** Every climate, every coast, every tavern, talking. Most are **Uomini Comuni** — Common Men, the great warm majority, farmers and clerks and soldiers, decent if fed and terrifying in crowds. Sprinkled through them are the **Misti**, the mixed cosmopolitan sort — Cartosa is thick with them — who carry, they will tell you at length, a trace of the old blood. And the old blood is real: the **Primigeni**, the Primeval stock, whom legend says the powers made from **Fire** itself, which is why humans do everything at a sprint and regret it after. A dark god got a grip on the fire-folk long ago, says the tale, and spoiled most; the remnants that kept their spirit are few and ferocious. Two you may actually meet: the **Vorskgen**, the hill-clans of the Nortmaar — ancestor-worshipping berserkers, generous past sense to a guest and unforgiving past sense to a thief (I once spent a winter with the Vorskgen; I still owe them a goat, and the goat is how I know the second fact) — and the **Ophiur**, a whole jungle-nation in the south: tall, very black-skinned, old-blooded especially in their aristocracy, speakers of Ophirita, admirers of Xebechani wisdom — patient people, then, since that is like admiring a glacier. Beyond these, men come in regional flavours — the **Norsi** of Helgedad's Winter Forest with their totem-spirits, **Hillmen**, **Woodmen**, the **High Men** — each sure the next valley over eats babies. Feed a human, admire his children, never touch his hat.

**The Xebechani.** Now I lower my voice, and you will lower yours. The eldest people: reptile-blooded, milk-pale, double-jointed, cool to the touch and cooler in conversation; they see the heat of your body, so do not bother lying to one about whether you are afraid. Four castes, fixed at birth by the scale-patterns on the back: the **Xudra**, massive and most reptilian, more than half of all their kind, never met abroad because they never leave; the **Xidhe**, the silk-voiced travelling caste, the only Xebechani most folk ever see, with a gaze like a hook in warm honey; the **Xion**, the priests; and the **Xattva**, the ethereal nobles and mages — meet one abroad and you have met an exile, so be gentle. They do not marry, they do not breed; when a Xebechani dies the body goes into **le Pozze**, the Pools, and from the Pools, by the rite called **la Rinascita**, comes a newborn. And the Pools — keep your voice down — are failing. Nobody living knows how they work; some children come out wrong now, or do not come out. The eldest people on Ymir is quietly, politely dying, and it does not speak of it, and neither will you at their table. Even I do not joke here. Trade honestly, keep your hands folded, and never steal from a temple with Pools in it. I know of what I speak.

> *The cat lowers her voice; lower yours. The Pozze are not mysteries, they are machines — the
> bioscience of the engineers the Xebechani name **gli Antichi**, who are the **Adepti** of these
> margins, still running after thousands of years with no one alive who can read them. The
> Rinascita is a recycler's liturgy. The race is not cursed and is not dying of sin; it is out of
> maintenance. When the last Pool fouls, the eldest people ends — of a broken machine, and only
> these margins will know the cause of death.*

**Dwarves.** Short, wide, bearded, and richer than you. The mountain kind are the pattern: smiths and engineers wanted in every court, builders of steam-engines, and sole holders of the secret of **Polvere Nera**, the black powder — the one secret on Ymir nobody has managed to steal, and believe me, the attempt has been made by professionals. Their plains cousins, the **Nani di Pianura**, went down among the humans for work; call one *Laak-nama-Khazalim* in earshot of a mountain dwarf and you will learn it is an insult, and then you will learn what dwarven dentistry costs. In the Cartosan jungles live the **Arawak** — dark-skinned, violet-eyed, who work stone and bone and never metal, braid gems into their hair to count their deeds, and prefer death to capture; their mountains hold **Shald**, the magical glass, and the expeditions that go after it feed the jungle. Dwarves are suspicious of my kind on principle, which is hurtful, and, in fairness, evidence-based. Pay a dwarf on time. There is no second tip.

**Elves.** Beautiful, tragic, and here by accident — they came through the world-doors in the Demon Wars, and stayed. Get the shape of them right, because they mind: there are three **kinds** — the **Alti**, high and haughty; the **Silvani**, most of them, wood-folk; the **Grigi**, the Grey, who are the Sea-elves and the only ones who will reliably drink with you — and, cutting clean across the kinds, three **creeds**. The **Chiari** wait in Alfheim behind the walls of Ost-en-Galad for the Last Messenger to lead them home to Niethel. The **Illuminati** — the other two call them Rinuncianti, quitters — live scattered among us and say the Sacred Realm is inside each elf, which makes them the pleasant ones. The **Oscuri**, the Dark Elves of the Gaerwath, say the Sacred Realm is Ymir, all of it, and that the rest of us are furniture. Any kind can hold any creed; there is no telling a Dark Elf by his face, only by his filing system: **Elar**, true elves; **Nemyel**, failed elves, outcast and rightless; **Kelvar**, everything else that breathes, which is you, which is me. I once smuggled a Nemyel out of the Gaerwath in a salt-fish barrel — she had failed their child-trial and been set to scraping **Arvo**, the magic-dust, for demon-dealers, and she wept the whole crossing, quietly, so the fish would not inform. I did not charge her. Put that in your book: once, the cat did not charge. And know this, for it is the sharpest gossip on the continent: on Ymir the elves *die*. Two hundred years, more or less, and out — whatever kept them deathless at home does not reach here, and the priests of Letia still argue whether they have souls at all, which the elves find as charming as you would.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *Roll-call, since she has now toured most of the stock. The dwarves were shaped on **Omalgat**,
> servant-work of the Omungus. The orcs are the makers' bio-soldiers of **Gwart**, run wild and
> downhill for an age. The Folletti were seeded from **Seelie**. Shaped — but not hollow: each
> people's own myth keeps its kernel of truth, for the making was done to living stock with
> histories of its own, and Durkaam and the Ancestors remember truly in a crooked mirror. The
> elves alone among the incomers were never made at all: native to **Tchyo**, hunted off it, they
> fled here through the makers' own doors during the Demon Wars. Niethel is no paradise lost to
> sin. It is Tchyo, before the hunt.*

**The Folletti**, the Little People, smallest and maybe rarest of the pure peoples, out of the Silver Isles — and yes, their capital is **Elendor**, where the Council of Peoples sits, which means the smallest folk host the biggest table, and they think that is very funny. Two sorts, and do not confuse them. The **Fate** — they call themselves **Tylweth Teg** — come in both sexes, whatever the ballads claim, stand under three feet, build toy-perfect cities deep in the woods, distrust the Tall Folk, are honestly baffled by dwarves, and hate everything that smells of the Maelstrom with a hatred out of all proportion to their size. The **Gnomi** are not their menfolk — say that twice and you have insulted two peoples with one sentence — but a separate race entirely, whose defining trait is wanting to be left alone, including by other Gnomi. A Gnomo's hospitality is a door that opens once. Take the once.

**My own people next, and I will be fair, which is more than they deserve.** We Tanoth come in two branches and two only. The **Tal-Tanoth** — myself — small cat-folk of the cities, needing our eight hours of sleep and not much else, living cheerfully off the humans, who call us a plague when they are angry and a nuisance when they are fond. We are the world's finest thieves, guides, wanderers, and finders of mislaid property, and if Ymir had halflings we would have eaten their lunch anyway. Men are big dolts; we love them the way you love a purse left open. And then there are the **Ur-Tanoth**, our great cousins — man-height and half again, furred like panther or tiger or lion by tribe, and despite the look, the gentlest souls on this world: shy nomad hunters, peerless workers of wood, gatherers of healing herbs and furs and rarities no city-hand could find. They shun the towns, and only we Tal can deal between them and you, so mind the tariff. And hear me now, because I stop joking exactly twice in this chapter and this is the second time: **an Ur-Tanoth caged is an Ur-Tanoth dead.** They fear the loss of the open sky past all reason; pen one and it sickens and dies in a season, every time. And still the beast-shows buy them, and still the "collectors" pay. I have opened three of those cages with my own hands, and I will open every one I find until I die, and if that costs me the Curator's respectable ink, let it. Buy Ur-Tanoth work at honest prices. Never buy an Ur-Tanoth.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The cat jokes that men are her cousins. Do not laugh yet. In the makers' registers the worlds
> are listed each with its stock — dwarf-lines for Omalgat, soldier-lines for Gwart, fairy-lines
> for Seelie — and nowhere in all the catalogue is there an entry for Man, nor for the Tanoth,
> because Ymir's own line reads only: homeworld of the Tanoth. The Adepti made much and remade
> more, but men and the Tanoth-kin they never made. These were already underfoot — the native
> stock of the makers' own seat, the unmade peoples on the workshop's doorstep. Whether Man is
> the Tanoth-line grown tall and bare, or the cat-folk are Man's elder cousins gone to fur, the
> registers do not trouble to distinguish: they are entered as one column. Mark what follows.
> Every made people's myth remembers its maker in a mask. Man has no maker to remember — which is
> why his myths are the muddiest, and his claim to this world the only one no forge can dispute.*

**The Sohleugir**, the lizard-folk of the southern seas, whose element is the water and whose manners are better than yours. Their cities of stone and coral climb in terraces above the waves and below them, and their deep capital, **Ssas'Kher**, has coral walls that have never once been breached — kingdoms of men should blush. They surface to trade pearls and coral, seldom for politics, and they are the fairest merchants and the most frightening soldiers I know. They know their faces alarm the warm-bloods — some of them believe it themselves, an old curse in the blood, poor dears — so they go veiled and hooded among us out of *courtesy*, if you please. Lift a Sohleugir's veil and you deserve whatever arrives. They despise the Gratar and all the corrupt races, and they pay in pearls, promptly, which is the whole of my religion.

**The Sarradhi** of the Sadhir — lizardfolk also, and if you call them Sohleugir's cousins to either party, enjoy the silence. Desert matriarchs, "Those Who Walk the Cycle": mystics and elemental mages who rule their Sultanate by the creed of the eternal **Wheel of Life**, in which everything returns, including — they will note, watching you — debts. The grandmothers run everything; charm the grandmother or leave. Under them serve the hermit-crab folk of the dried inland sea, guides who can take you where nothing else can. The desert chapter of this book is theirs by right; I only add — bring a gift with a history. They can tell.

**Now the jungle south, and your life may hang on the difference.** The **Nkishi**, the wild orcs of the deep Cartosan green, are the bravest things on legs: from childhood they let beasts bite them and wear the scars as a book of deeds, and their **Bokkor** shamans treat with the ancestors and the dark forest gods, the **Orisha** — the worst of whom, the **Mayombe**, you should not name after sundown, and I notice it is sundown, so we move on. Meet Nkishi: stand still, do not flinch, show your own scars if you have them. Courage is the coin; cowards are cargo. The **M'Bantha** goblins of the jungle-edge are another animal entirely: they believe humans keep their magic — they say **"Cargo"** — inside their everyday things, so they steal the things to steal the power, and go jingling with trinkets hammered out of stolen kettles. People call them stupid. People are wrong at spear-range: they are patient, clever ambushers. My survival kit for M'Bantha country is a bright brass compass that has never pointed north in its life, carried where it can be admired. Rich in Cargo, poor in loss. You are welcome.

**The half-peoples.** Where peoples meet, children happen, and the learned count sixteen documented crossings, each with a technical name and a common name, and the common name is usually the cruel one. You know the everyday ones — the **Yafysh** half-elves, the **Ghzaak** half-orcs, common as coppers in the Qi-Long. Of the rest, the vivid ones: the **Changeling**, fairy-blood in a human line, lovely and short-lived as a rumour; the **Hobgoblin**, goblin crossed with orc, which breeds true and drills in ranks, worse luck; the **Dwergar**, the "foul dwarf," goblin-and-dwarf, hated by both grandmothers and harder than either; the **Gremlin**, kobold-and-fairy, small, spiteful, and the finest lockwrights I have ever been professionally embarrassed by; the **Kappa** of the warm shallows, lizard-blood gone wrong, that will drown a swimmer for etiquette; and the sad **Uomo-Pesce**, the Fish-Man, of a human and one of the Rahab of the deep. There are more — Tanoth-crossed lines among them, and I do not discuss those at supper — and the only rule worth ink is: the half-peoples catch stones from both sides of their parentage, so if one serves you loyally, you have hired the most loyal thing on the road. It has nowhere else to be.

**Last, the touched and the twisted, and one more flash of temper.** The **Marchiati**, the Marked, are not a people but a lottery: children touched in the womb by the Maelstrom, born with deformities and, sometimes, with gifts that make magisters go pale. Most villages smother them in the cradle. I have guided two Marchiati over the mountains in my time, and both were kinder company than the villages that wanted them dead, so you may have my opinion of the custom without charge. The **Ibridi** — the Maelstrom's older children — are peoples of a sort: **Centauri** on the steppes, goat-legged **Satiri** who will drink you into a ditch and lament you from the ditch's edge, **Tritoni** in the green water, **Giganti** in the high waste with old elemental fire in some of their lines. Treat each as you find it; the category is a fence humans built, and the fence leaks. But the **Vulfen** and the **Gratar** — the degenerate races, bred-down servants of the Sovereign Demons — I have met only at spearpoint, and so has everyone who has met them twice. I make no exceptions in this book because I have never been shown one. If a Gratar offers you terms, count your fingers, then run.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *Why do the wise fear the Marchiati, when the village priest fears only a crooked child? Because
> the Maelstrom is not weather. It is the wound over drowned Ragash, and what bleeds from it is
> the Corrupted Essence — and a Marked child is a place where the wound has signed its name inside
> the world's own stock. The villages kill what looks demon-touched and are wrong about the demon.
> The wise count differently: each Marked cradle is the seam grown thinner, the Tide arriving
> early, one child at a time. Killing them does not thicken the seam. Nothing does.*

There. Fifteen suppers' worth of the world — the Curator says nine; the Curator was not counting the ones I expensed elsewhere. Every people on this list will feed you, and every one will kill you, and the whole craft of the road is knowing which door you knocked on. Learn their food, their insults, and their prices, in that order. And if a small cat-shaped shadow shares your fire some night and asks what that shiny thing is — say it is Cargo, and hold it tighter.
