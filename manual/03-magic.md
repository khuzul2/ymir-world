# III · OF THE ESSENCE
## *a Treatise on Magic: its Substance, its Realms, its Corruption, and its Control*

**As set down by Sir Drusilde, Archmage and Directress of the College of the Seventh Circle of Armora, in the year 3027 d.M. — the second of the Age of the Tide — being the doctrinal preface to her *Red Book of Magical Control*.**

> *Curator's note.* Five centuries separate us from this treatise, and no one has replaced it.
> Drusilde wrote it as the opening of her *Red Book* — the "Vademecum of Practical Anti-Magic"
> that Armora's Section for Magical Control still issues to every recruit — and it remains the
> standard work on the Essence. Read her with two cautions. First, she wrote a bare two years
> after the Tide broke: what is settled history to us was, to her, an emergency in progress.
> Second, she was trained by Xochiyayotl of Xuthal — the priest whose cosmogony opens this
> Compendium — and spent her life politely refusing to believe it. Where the teacher offered a
> mystery, the pupil reached for a scale and weights. Both, I have come to think, were holding
> one end of the same animal. — *the Curator*

---

The reader will forgive me a definition before a single wonder. Wonders mislead; definitions do
not. This preface exists because the Grand Duke's commission asked my College for a book to let an
ordinary soldier survive an extraordinary enemy, and a soldier cannot counter what his officers
cannot define. Neither, I have found, can most wizards.

### I. Of the Substance

**Observation the first: magic is a substance before it is an art.** The learned call it
**the Essence**; the vulgar say **mana**, and I permit the word in my lecture-halls
because it is short. The Essence fills and underlies creation as water fills a sponge —
impersonal, inexhaustible on any scale we can measure, indifferent to the uses we make of it. The
**Spirits**, those immaterial dwellers of the Ethereal, move through it and are in some manner
*of* it; every working of magic, from a hedge-witch's charm to the drowned engines of the Adepts,
is a displacement of this one substance.

**Observation the second: the substance precipitates.** Where the Maelstrom's **Resonance** beats
against the world, the Essence settles out as a fine physical sediment called **Arvo** — powdered
magic, as silt is powdered mountain. The Dark Elves prize it beyond gold. My College keeps a
dram in a case lined with Orihalcum, and I have watched it for twenty years the way other women
watch the sea.

My first teacher — Xochiyayotl of Xuthal, to whom I owe my method and one persistent headache —
would tell you that the Essence is **Ech, the One**, a held breath older than the gods, and would
say it in a manner that made the candles feel cold. I honour that being too much to flatter it:
this is poetry. What I can *attest* is that the substance exists; that it can be detected (my
College's **Essence-meter** does nothing else), stored, conducted, amplified, nullified, and —
the burden of this whole book — **denied to those who would misuse it**. Whether it is also a god
I leave to the priests, who at least are paid for it.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The teacher told her the truth and she filed it under poetry. The Essence IS what the old
> priest calls Ech — the substrate beneath the whole cosmogony of Chapter I — and Drusilde's
> instinct to meter it rather than kneel to it is not new. It is the oldest instinct on Ymir. The
> **Adepts** worked the Essence with machines, precisely as she dreams of doing, and it made them
> gods in fact if not in right. Her College is not founding a science. It is excavating one.*

### II. Of the Three Realms, and of the Arcane

Practitioners of every stripe we call **Channelers**; the word is deliberately
unromantic. What separates them is not the substance, which is one, but the **conduit** through
which they draw it. There are **three base realms**, and a fourth thing that is not a fourth
realm.

**Essence** — the realm named for the substance itself, because it draws the Essence *raw* from
the world: the arcane and elemental tradition, the fireball and the ward, the bread and butter of
Cartosa's **Blue Mantles** and above all of their **Elementalists** of Water and Wind, who are
genuinely excellent and will tell you so unprompted. Its strength is force — no other realm
matches it for elemental violence. Its weaknesses are practical and beautiful: it wants the
spoken word and the free gesture, and it despises armour. The *Red Book*'s chapters on gags,
manacles, and wet sand are the least glamorous pages I have ever written, and have saved more
lives than any battle-mage in the Grand Duke's pay.

**Mentalism** — magic channelled *through the mind*, the mind serving as catalyst rather than
source. This is the subtle realm: the Mentalist casts without gesture and without voice, and the
only outward sign is a faint reddening of the eyes — which is why the *Red Book* teaches every
checkpoint sergeant to look travellers in the face. A Mentalist fights the way a stiletto fights:
illusion, assault upon the mind, the bending of passions, the quiet strengthening of the self,
levitation and short translocations. He does not fight the way a regiment fights, and that is how
you beat him — his arts favour himself and take one victim at a time, and they are poor cousins
at elemental force. Watch the eyes; do not parley; come in numbers. A crossbow at eighty paces is
entirely indifferent to a stare.

**The Flow** — the divine realm: power not learned but *granted*, by the gods, to their
priests, on terms no mortal audits. I state as doctrine what my colleagues only mutter: this is
the realm the regulator cannot reach. Cartosa registers and taxes every Essence-user in the
Principality, but — as the *Red Book* has it — *no one has yet dared ask the priests to
register.* The gods answer to no guild, and their servants answer to the gods. The Section's
guidance on hostile clergy is accordingly the most careful writing in this book, and I summarise
it in one line: you are not fighting the priest.

**The Arcane** — and here let the lesser colleges attend, for most print this wrongly — is **not a
fourth source**. It is the **union of the three base realms**: the highest, rarest, most
versatile discipline, in which one Channeler weds the raw draw of Essence, the silent conduit
of Mentalism, and something uncomfortably like the granted grace of the Flow. I have met four true
Arcanists in my life. Two frightened me. The Arcane Academy of Cartosa advertises a curriculum
in it, which is charming.

### III. Of Corruption, which is not a School

The gravest error in the popular understanding — and in three textbooks I could name — is to
speak of the "dark arts" as a fourth realm with its own students and syllabus. **Corruption is
not a school. It is a taint**, and it can afflict any realm as rot can afflict any timber. The
necromancer channelling **Dark Flows**, the demon-binder drawing on **Dark Essence**, the
Mentalist who has bargained his dreams away — these **Corrupted Channelers** did not matriculate
somewhere; they were *contaminated*, by the substance scholars call the Corrupted Essence, whose
wellspring is Ahriman's and whose handling marks the handler. My College grades that
contamination on a ledger the profane have shortened to the **CEO** — *Corruzione da Essenza
Oscura*, in the old tongue: **Dark-Essence Corruption** — and we grade it in anyone, of any
realm, of any rank, because it respects none. It
accrues the way soot accrues: from demon-wrought gear, from pacts, from sustained work with the
dark derivations of the elements. In the civilised south its two ripest expressions,
**necromancy** and **demon-summoning**, are capital matters — whispered of all the same in
certain great houses, and practised openly and with terrible refinement by the Dark Elves of the
Gaerwath.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *She is righter than her Church and righter than her Grand Duke. The Corrupted Essence is not
> Ahriman's malice; it is **spoilage** — the portion of the Essence that curdled when the Adepts'
> theft-ritual tore the flow of it at Ragash. The demons poured out of that curdling like flies
> out of a wound. Every point on Drusilde's tidy ledger measures the leakage of a single ancient
> crime, and the "capital matter" her state executes people for is trafficking in the by-product
> of the theft her own gods committed.*

### IV. Of the Elements and their Derivations

The Essence, drawn raw, expresses itself along **six Elements** — **Fire, Water, Ice, Air, Earth,
and Light** — each presided over by one of the Six of the cosmogony and each crowned, below the
gods, by a semi-divine **Elemental Lord**: Diis in the flame, Hudemia in the water, Varshnak
in the ice, Heis in the wind, Therìos in the earth, and Dingir in the light and its shadow, for
the sixth element carries its own negation within it. Elementalism — the disciplined working of
one element — is the strong right arm of the Essence realm and the most easily licensed, because
its effects are at least visible.

Beyond the six lie the **derivations**: hybrid and negative expressions that the six produce as
surely as two notes produce a chord. The field officer must know four — **Storm**, the violent
marriage of air and water; **Nether**, the cold undertow of the Essence itself, Ahriman's charge
upon the substance; **Spectral**, where the Essence touches the Ethereal and the dead; and
**Umbra**, which some colleges call simply the Dark, light's devouring negative. These are not
seventh elements, whatever the Helgedad skalds sing of storm-wyrms; they are compounds and
inversions, and they answer to the same counters as their parents, only less politely.

Separate from all of these stands the **Void** — the domain of **Topramesk the
Yellow**, that most singular and least worshipped of powers. The Void is not an element and not
the substance: it is the *absence* into which substance can be unmade. I insist on the
distinction because the Academy of Cartosa still prints "mana" for both Topramesk's domain and
the common stuff of magic, and I have written to them twice. Reserve *mana* for the substance.
What Topramesk keeps is the hole.

### V. Of the Maelstrom's Grip

All doctrine of magic on Ymir is doctrine *under the Maelstrom*, and the officer who forgets it
will die surprised. The great vortex lays four hands on our art:

1. **The Outflow.** Free translocation — the teleport of the storybooks — decays with distance.
   Within a town it is merely dangerous; beyond a modest range the failures multiply upon each
   other until no sane Channeler attempts it, which is why no power on Ymir moves armies or
   cargo by spell, and why my harbour-masters still have employment.
2. **The Obscuring.** Divination is clouded. No scrying pierces the Maelstrom itself, and
   visions grow unreliable as they reach into the far past or future. Trust no seer who claims
   otherwise; bill any who insist.
3. **The Arcane Storms.** The Central Ocean is periodically wracked by storms of raw magical
   energy channelling forces from beyond the world. The Section's standing order in an Arcane
   Storm is a sailor's: sea-room, and none of your cleverness.
4. **The Mark.** The Maelstrom's energy sometimes touches the unborn, and the **Marked**
   are the result — twisted in body, gifted in ways that fit no realm cleanly. Most do not
   survive their villages. Those who reach my College are studied, paid, and — I record it as
   policy — protected.

To this grim list the present age has added the exception that has upended everything: **the
portals**. Understand the distinction, for it is the hinge of our era. The ancient gates — the
Adepts' own works, and the **True Ways** by which the Xebechani revived their network fifteen
centuries ago — are **fixed-gate constructions**, and the Outflow does not touch them: the warp
that ruins free teleportation was never their prison. What held *them* shut was a seal — the
isolation the chroniclers call the bell — and two years ago that seal broke, and every gate on
Ymir woke at once. Free translocation remains as ruined as ever; the fixed doors stand open. I
have therefore recommended to the Grand Duke that warders be posted at every known gate, and I
commend the same to whatever governments read this: **a door that cannot be moved can be
watched**, and had the ancients watched theirs, this book might not have been necessary.

> *Curator's note.* It was necessary. Five hundred years on, the picture is exactly as she drew
> it: the Outflow holds, the seers still cannot pierce the vortex, and the gates of the True Ways
> stand open and garrisoned from Elendor to Qi-Long, carrying the trade of the New Equilibrium.
> Her "gate-warders," proposed as an emergency measure, are now so universal an institution that
> most readers will be startled to learn a single woman had to argue for them. — *the Curator*

### VI. Of the Regulation of Magic

Magic in the civilised south is practised openly and mistrusted thoroughly, which is the correct
arrangement. Its lawful economy is fourfold — **protection** (wards, golems, the bodyguard-mage),
**travel** (the navigation-arts that keep Cartosa rich), **healing** (temple and lay, costly and
rationed), and **entertainment** (licensed, lest the illusionists discover politics). Its
governance takes two great forms:

- **The Cartosan model.** Every user of Essence, Mentalism, or the Arcane in the Principality must
  register with the **Hermetic Order of the Blue Mantles** and pay the annual dues, or stand
  outlaw. Priests are exempt (see the Flow, above) and nobles are exempt (see human nature). A tax
  collected by wizards from wizards, and it works better than it deserves to.
- **The Young Kingdoms' model — the permit, Levels A through D.** We license the *act*, not the
  person: **A** for lawful practice without guild membership; **B** for temporary leave to work
  arts normally forbidden, or to bring a summoned creature within city walls; **C** for the same
  at greater breadth and duration; **D** — near-total licence — which only a prince of the Young
  Kingdoms may grant, and which I have countersigned exactly three times.

And beneath both stands my own house: the **Section for Magical Control**, Armora's anti-magic
corps, which takes ordinary soldiers — not one caster among them, by design — and trains them to
recognise, disrupt, and survive each realm in turn. The S.C.M. is the reason the *Red Book*
exists; my College supplies the theory, the Section the bruises. Lesser colleges call the
enterprise unsporting. They are correct, and that is the point: sport is for duellists, and the
Essence is not a duel. It is weather, and we are building roofs.

### VII. Of Materials

The quartermaster's page. Commit these to memory; the examinations are not gentle.

- **Shald** — violet volcanic glass; a living reservoir of Essence that refills itself and can be
  drained by a working mage. Its refined form, **Actorios**, is the summoner's stone.
- **Orihalcum** — nullifies all magic, without appeal. An Orihalcum-lined cell is worth forty
  interrogators; the Section's manacles have it at the wrist.
- **Thalaras** — the opposite number: amplifier and conductor of the Essence, priced accordingly.
- **Umbra** — a light-devouring material from another world, come to us through the Night Folk. It
  corrupts toward the Dark. We do not stock it. Do not bring me any.
- **Demon Steel** — which taints its bearer toward Ahriman; and **Dream Steel** — mined
  beneath the Dream World, whose virtue waxes with the
  bearer's conviction. The one corrupts the faithful and the other rewards them.
- **Black Powder** — the dwarves' secret, whose making no other race has reconstructed.
  Not, strictly, magical — and the most closely guarded substance on this list even so.
- **Alchemical Coal** — char of Urakron wood, which drives an engine three times as
  long as the common sort. The unglamorous foundation of half of Armora's marvels, and of every
  tram my critics ride to their lectures against magitech.

### VIII. Of Famous Instruments

I end with the instruments the age will be remembered by, because two years ago six of them
remade the world.

**The six Relics** — **Ignus, Saralis, Meletskar** (which the easterners call the
**Masamune**), **Tassalung, Natharien** the Celestial Diadem, and **Oedring** — are not, as the
ballads already have it, six holy treasures. They are **six seals upon four Magic Runes**: four
engines of the Adepts, housed from time immemorial in the Xebechani fortress-city of **Xanadu**,
and keyed — I record the correspondence with a scholar's shiver — one to each realm of magic, the
Arcane included. Four Runes; six locks. The Adepts did not build half-measures. When the Relics
were gathered and the Runes unsealed at the dawn of this era, that single act was what shattered
the Maelstrom's isolation — the unsealing and the breaking of the bell are **one event**,
whatever the pamphleteers print. I have handled exactly one of the six: Meletskar, for the better
part of an afternoon, with its bearer's leave and both my hands shaking. It is the finest made
thing I have ever touched, and I include my own Essence-meters in the comparison, which costs me
something to write.

Of **the Door of Despair** — with its Forgotten Scrolls and its
terrible Key — I say only that it is a separate dread, no kin to the Runes, and that I decline to
write more of it in a book meant for soldiers.

Of the named blades the reader will meet in the histories — **Soul-Reaper** (*Mietianime*), forged they say by
the death-god himself and carried by Morannagul of the Gaerwath; the Masamune above; and
**Oblivion**, the axe of Tofango — I note only the last as doctrine. Within Oblivion a mighty
demon is imbued and trapped, and its will gnawed its wielder's mind until the greatest axeman of
his age ended his days a mad hermit on a mountaintop. The case of Tofango is the whole of my
third chapter in a single life: **corruption is a property of the instrument and the handling,
not of the man's realm or his virtue** — which is why my Section registers artefacts as
diligently as it registers wizards.

> *Curator's note.* The unsealing that was two years old when Drusilde wrote is five centuries
> old as I copy her, and the world it opened is the one we live in. Where the four Runes stand
> now, and in whose keeping, is matter for the historical chapters. Her "case of Tofango" is
> still taught, in her exact words, in every college from Armora to the Academy she teased.
> They forgave her. It took a while. — *the Curator*

Magic, then, is not a mystery. It is a substance; substances can be measured; and what can be
measured can be governed. That conviction built my College, armed my Section, and wrote this
book. Yet I close with the honesty I demand of my students. My old teacher called the Essence a
held breath, and after forty years of weighing it I cannot prove the old priest wrong — only
unhelpful. So let the doctrine stand, and one line of poetry beside it: if the Essence should
prove after all to be a breath, then let us learn, before it exhales, *who is breathing*.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *Nearer than she knows, at the last. Her beloved instruments — the meters, the gates, the Runes
> she venerates as the summit of "magic control" — are the **toys of the usurpers**: the four
> Runes are the Adepts' anti-demon weapons, built to scour the spoilage of their own theft, and the
> "isolation bell" she mourns as a broken protection was **their seal** — the lid the thieves
> screwed down over the wound, the demons, the gates, and the sleeping gods alike. What the
> heroes of the Tide performed at Xanadu was not a liberation or a catastrophe but a **key
> turning**, ages late, in a door the owners never meant to stay shut. Drusilde spent her life
> perfecting the discipline of watching doors. She never learned that the finest were built by
> the burglars.*
