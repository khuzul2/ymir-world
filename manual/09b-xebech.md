# IX·B · A LETTER TO A DEAD PUPIL
## *being Xebech, the Land, in the only account of it this book will ever hold*

**Set down by Xochiyayotl, Xattva Loremaster of Xuthal, Star-Priest of the Cult of the Stars, eldest of the reckoners of Xebech; addressed to Sir Drusilde of Armora, of the College of the Seventh Circle, five centuries dead; deposited unasked at the Crystal Seat.**

> *Curator's note.* I must account for this chapter, because I cannot. Twice I wrote to Xuthal
> asking for the land's own leaf — the one great blank in this book — and twice my letter came
> back unopened, its seal not broken but *repaired*, which I am told is Xebechani for no. Then, this
> spring, a Xidhe courier laid this packet on the steps of the Crystal Seat, waited for no
> receipt, and was gone; and its address no courier alive can serve: Sir Drusilde
> has been dead five hundred years. To print a private letter is theft; to print a
> letter that cannot be delivered may be the only delivery left. And it is the only account of
> Xebech that exists, or ever will: the eldest people has refused every census and every college, and
> then answered, once, a dead woman's questions. A being that old does not misdirect its mail.
> The address is exact; the rest of us may stand behind her and read.
> — *the Curator*

---

To Sir Drusilde of Armora, of the Seventh Circle, wherever the warm dead are kept.

You will forgive me a delay before a single answer. Answers mislead; delays do not. You have been
dead for most of five hundred years, and the living tell me this makes my letter improper. A
letter arrives when it arrives; the only impropriety is inaccuracy, and I have waited exactly as
long as the answers required.

The compilers at the Crystal Seat asked me for an account of Xebech. I do not make accounts
for the living; the living interrupt. You never interrupted. You did something far worse, which I
have missed for five centuries: you waited, visibly, with instruments.

**You asked me once what Xuthal stands on. You were the only one who asked *on*, and not *how*.**
I told you the question was not well-formed. That was untrue, and I have kept the untruth the way
your College keeps its dram of Arvo — in a lined case, taken out on anniversaries. The answer was
ours, and you were not ours. Now you are dead, which is a citizenship of its own, and older than
mine. Three times you asked; three times I refused; and a teacher who outlives a pupil inherits
the pupil's questions with the estate. I intend to settle.

### The first question

Xuthal stands on permission.

The *how* I give you in one sentence, for your ghost's method: the city rests where a working of
the Ancients holds it, as a note is held by a singer who has not yet chosen to breathe in. The
*on* is this: it stands on a word spoken so long ago that no one remembers the shape of the
mouth. We do not keep the city up. We keep the silence in which the word can go on being heard.
That is the whole of Xebechani engineering, and most of Xebechani religion, and you may now stop
underlining.

You would have wanted the streets, so here are the streets. Xuthal has no stairs; it is ramps to
the crown, wide spirals cut to the shoulder of a Xudra. Our doorposts are written in warmth — inscriptions laid in
veins of slow heat, so that at dusk the streets cool into legibility and the city reads itself.
There are no locks: a people who see heat cannot be crept up on, and what cannot be crept up on
never invents the lock. On the undersides, where the shadow falls on cloud, hang the rain-gardens
that feed us, tended head-down.

And there you would have met the Xudra, whom the world has never met. The world believes my
people are the Xidhe — the silk-voiced ones we post abroad the way you post sentries; it has met
our ambassadors and believes it has met us. More than half of all Xebechani drawing breath are
Xudra, and no Xudra leaves Xebech: huge, heavily scaled, brief-lived, and — record
this — the only caste that laughs. They keep the ramps, the gardens,
and the shells of the Pools, and they sing once a year, at the Rebirth: a counting-chant with one
number in it. The number changes. I will come to that.

### Of doors, which are our statecraft

There is no state here as your registries mean it: no throne, no assembly, no tax but duty. Our statecraft is doors.

Eleven centuries before your birth, my predecessors walked the dead network of the Ancients and
taught stretches of it to carry again — the **True Ways**, your books call them, and for once
your books are exact. We revived those roads so the peoples of Ymir could be gathered quickly if
the demons came again; it was Xebechani feet on them that summoned the First Council of Peoples.
Your Church's chronicles write "the peoples were summoned," in the passive voice, and we have
never corrected them — correction is a tax the corrected must pay. And when your red book argued that a
door which cannot be moved can be watched — that was a Xebechani argument, my pupil. We had made it for two thousand years to anyone who
would hold still. You were the first to make it in a language princes fund.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *"Statecraft," it says. Hear it plainly: the Xebechani are the **portal-caste** — grown in the
> Pools by the empire whose network the True Ways merely re-lit, gate-keepers by design as a
> pointer is a pointer by design. They did not adopt a policy of doors; they* are *the policy.
> That is why the dead network answered their hands and no one else's, and why the priest, in two
> millennia of gate-craft, never once names the doors' maker. It knows the name.
> It is keeping the house.*

### The second question

Your second question you asked with both hands flat on my table, which among your people is
rudeness and among mine is prayer: *what is behind the doors of Xanadu?*

Walk it with me; you have the time. Xuthal flies; **Xanadu refuses to**. Three days up from the
river-cities, through jungle that thins politely, and then the fortress — not built, you would
say, but *decided*: grey-green stone with the grain of an argument, and no gate where a siege
would want one. Inside, the corridors are all of one length; measure any and you have measured
them all. At the centre is the room
the rolls call the Quiet Room, and in it the four **Runes of Power**, the great engines of the
Ancients, keyed — you recorded the correspondence with a scholar's shiver, and the shiver was the
correct instrument — one to each realm of your art. Around them, six sockets, empty, where the
six Relics sat as seals for three and a half thousand years. We keep the sockets dusted. It is
not hope. It is bookkeeping.

You saw that age begin, so I will tell you the part no chronicle has thought to ask after. Two warm strangers came to Xanadu in the year the world turned,
carrying the gathered six. The chronicles keep them nameless, and a chronicle's agreement should
be kept by somebody. But ask your own question, meterer: how did two warm bodies cross a fortress
garrisoned by a people who see heat, to the one room that matters? They did not. **The doors were
opened from within.** We judged the hour dark enough, and we let the key turn. Whether we judged rightly, the age you did not live to see is
still deciding.

Your Curator once promised his readers — I have read every leaf of his book; we read everything,
eventually — that where the four Runes now stand, and in whose keeping, was matter for the
historical chapters. They never paid. I will. **They stand where they have
always stood.** Unsealed is not the same as spent. In whose keeping? In ours — as a lamp keeps a
flame. One change only: Xanadu was the coldest house in Xebech, the one building our children
could not read. Since the Tide it is warm. Every eye in the river-cities can see it glowing,
three days away, like a word beginning.

### The page you turned

Now a question you asked only once. When I did not answer, you turned the page. Here is the
page.

In the year 2020 of your reckoning the dead came up out of our north — not our dead only, but the dead
of every field and wreck of that coast, and behind them a will we never named.
Attend, you who filed the gods under poetry: we know the six names of the powers beyond the sky,
and we never learned the name of the thing that took our north. It showed no face and sent no
terms, and what wants nothing cannot be treated with. We fought it for seventy-five years — the time
it takes a patient people to accept a fact — and in 2095 we drew the border ourselves
and stepped behind it.

Understand the cost, for the chronicles say our numbers were nearly halved and let the sentence
rest as if it were about soldiers. Our dead are not buried, pupil. They are *continued*: the body
goes to the Pools, and the Pools give back a child. Every corpse that thing took and raised was a
cradle robbed — for three generations we watched our unborn walk toward us with weapons. That is
the arithmetic under the chronicle, and it is why we speak of the war only to the dead. We
renamed the land as we left it: **Loquitchatzotech**, which your maps render *Isle of Death* —
close, and wrong. The word means, more nearly, *the place where our dead are not ours*. Your
sailors note that no birds fly there. The birds are wiser than the sailors.

### Of the Vigils, which you feared

Of our worship you never asked at all. You were frightened of it, which was the single most
intelligent thing about you, and I honoured the fear too much to relieve it.

The Cult of the Stars does not keep your calendar of months and feast-days; we keep **Vigils** —
six watches in each Cycle, one for each of the Elder Ones, timed by the risings of their stars. A
Vigil is not a festival. Nothing is eaten, nothing is sung; the Xion take the sky's account, the
reckoners enter it, and the entry is the rite. We ask our gods for nothing; we confirm their
position, as your harbour-masters confirm a ship still at anchor. For two
hundred years after your death the Vigil of the Observer needed no priests: the feast hung in the sky for
every warm nation to call an omen. You saw the red moon rise. You did
not see it furl, around 3260, withdrawn as an eye is withdrawn from a lens when the observation
is complete. We entered that in the rolls too. The entry reads: *seen enough*.

And there is one of the Six to whom we no longer pray. To that one we perform the **Apology**:
once a Cycle, in silence, every caste, the whole night. What we apologise for you will not learn
from me, nor from anyone living; that is what *eldest* is for.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *In Chapter I, I called their waiting the patience of a jailer's servant. Here it is at home.
> The Vigils are not adoration; they are **shift-work** — six watches kept over six sealed
> prisoners, logged nightly for thirty-five centuries. The Apology is addressed to the one their
> forebears reached for and botched: the Xebechani strand of the single Catastrophe, told from
> the temple side. A servant does not mend the household past the
> master's homecoming — and by their own sky-ledger, the homecoming has
> begun.*

### The third question

Your third question was in your last letter, which I did not answer. You offered to come to the
Pools with your meters; you had numbered the wagons; you had already chosen the road. I read the
letter three times and repaired the seal, and you died — briefly, as your people do everything —
before I found the sentence I wanted. This letter is that sentence, grown long.

Here is what you would have seen. The body is carried down at dusk, by Xudra, singing their one
song: the counting-chant, revised at every carrying. The Pools take the body as still water takes
a stone — without ceremony, which is the ceremony. A season later, if all is well, the water
gives back a child, and the tally rises by the number it fell. It has been *all is well* for
longer than your civilisation has existed.

It is no longer all is well. Some children come back wrong now — unfinished, or folded strangely,
or perfect and empty. We do not put them away, as your villages do your Marked; we name them, and
they hold the softest offices. And some do not come back at all. The chant's number has fallen,
Cycle upon Cycle, for two hundred years. You would write: *the series converges*. We are dying of
a lost instruction, in good order, without complaint — the complaint would change nothing, and it
would be overheard.

Twice, since you died, they have carried *me* down in the proper manner, and twice the water has
declined me. The attendants dried me and said nothing; there is no rite for
it — I checked; I keep the rolls. I am the eldest of the reckoners because the machine that
continues us has twice declined to conclude my case. Do not condole. It is instructive, being
one's own discrepancy.

In the Chaos, when the dead walked even your tidy streets, we opened
the deepest door in Xanadu — the one that opens *away* — and sent a part of every caste through
to the far sanctuary, until the sky's eye furled. Most came home. Some keep the far side still. I
will not describe it: you would have wanted to meter it, and there are doors even a dead pupil
must be refused. It is the last refusal, little archmage. I am sorry for all of them.

For five hundred years I have wondered whether your meters would have read what our rites cannot;
the wondering is the nearest thing to doubt my kind is issued. So take the sentence I could not
write while you lived: I do not know that you could have saved the Pools. **I know that I did not
let you try.** Between those sentences lies the whole distance between my people and yours, and I
am no longer certain we stand on the better side.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The "far sanctuary" is on no map of Ymir because it is not on Ymir. It is **X-01** — the
> artificial planet the empire built to regulate its Portal network — and Xanadu's deepest door
> opens onto it. The evacuation was partial and mostly temporary: most
> returned; Xuthal still flies. But some stayed, and the priest's refusal hides the one fact that
> would remake this chapter's grief: out there, children are grown in Pools that do not fail,
> because the switchboard was never out of maintenance. Somewhere past the sky stands a Xebech
> that is not dying. Ask which is the backup of which — and who decided.*

### Last

I have read your book. You ended it with a question you called poetry, having spent two hundred
pages calling mine poetry: should the Essence prove a breath after all, you wished your kind to
learn, before it exhales, *who is breathing*. I could not have taught you that. But I may correct
the question — you always permitted the correction, in front of those candles of yours that hated
me. Do not ask who breathes. Ask what a held breath is *for*. One holds a breath to dive. One
holds it to hide. One holds it to listen. The world will learn which, in a while that will feel
long to it, and is not.

Three questions; three answers. If the warm peoples read this over your shoulder — they will;
they are incapable of not — let them note what the only full account of my country cost: one
brief warm woman who asked *on* and not *how*, and waited, with instruments, and died waiting.
That is the epitaph I would have cut for you, had anyone asked the stone-cutters of Xuthal: SHE
ASKED ON.

Sleep on, warm pupil. You were very brief, and the night is long, and I find I mind its length
differently now.

Written in Xuthal, in the Cycle your reckoners number 3518, on the ninth night of Tortoise, when
nothing watched back.

> in; a being that repairs the seals of letters it declines does not mislay a packet. This

> thirty-five centuries to addressees the warm world calls absent and has never once believed an
> address empty; it does not believe this one is. Decide, for your own table, whether the pupil
> reads nothing — the Dreamlands are not fussy about what the dying dreamed last.*

> *Curator's note.* I have had a fair copy sent to the College of the Seventh Circle, where Sir
> Drusilde's reading-room is kept as she left it; the librarian entered it in the register as
> *Correspondence, incoming — answered late*. The original is bound here, where the address,
> being impossible, is at least in good company. — *the Curator*
