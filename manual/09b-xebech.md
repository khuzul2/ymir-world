# IX·B · A LETTER TO A DEAD PUPIL
## *being Xebech, the Land, in the only account of it this book will ever hold*

**Set down by Xochiyayotl, Xattva Loremaster of Xuthal, Star-Priest of the Cult of the Stars, eldest of the reckoners of Xebech; addressed to Sir Drusilde of Armora, Archmage and Directress of the College of the Seventh Circle, five centuries dead; deposited unasked at the Crystal Seat.**

> *Curator's note.* I must account for this chapter, because I cannot account for it. Twice I
> wrote to Xuthal asking for the land's own leaf — the one great blank in this book — and twice
> my letter came back unopened, its seal not broken but *repaired*, which I am told is Xebechani
> for no. Then, this spring, a Xidhe courier laid the following packet on the steps of the
> Crystal Seat, waited for no receipt, and was gone; and it bears an address no courier alive can
> serve, for Sir Drusilde of Armora has been dead five hundred years. I sat with the impropriety
> a long while. To print a private letter is theft; to print a letter that cannot be delivered
> may be the only delivery left. What decided me was arithmetic, which she would have approved:
> this is the only account of Xebech that exists, or ever will. The eldest people has refused
> every census and every college — and then answered, once, a dead woman's questions. I do not
> believe a being that old misdirects its mail. I believe the address is exact, and that the rest
> of us are permitted to stand behind her and read. — *the Curator*

To Sir Drusilde of Armora, of the Seventh Circle, wherever the warm dead are kept.

You will forgive me a delay before a single answer. Answers mislead; delays do not. You have been
dead for most of five hundred years, and the living tell me this makes my letter improper. A
letter arrives when it arrives; the only impropriety is inaccuracy, and I have waited precisely
as long as the answers required.

The compilers at the Crystal Seat asked me for an account of my country. I do not make accounts
for the living; the living interrupt. You never interrupted. You did something far worse, which I
find I have missed for five centuries: you waited, visibly, with instruments.

**You asked me once what Xuthal stands on. You were the only one who asked *on*, and not *how*.**
I told you the question was not well-formed. That was untrue, and I have kept the untruth the way
your College keeps its dram of Arvo — in a lined case, taken out on anniversaries. The answer was
ours, and you were not ours. Now you are dead, which is a citizenship of its own, and older than
mine. Attend, then. Three times you asked and three times I refused, and a teacher who outlives a
pupil inherits the pupil's questions with the rest of the estate. I intend to settle it in full.

### The first question

Xuthal stands on permission.

The *how* I give you in one sentence, for your ghost's method: the city rests where a working of
the Ancients holds it, as a note is held by a singer who has not yet chosen to breathe in. The
*on* is this: it stands on a word spoken so long ago that no one remembers the shape of the
mouth. We do not keep the city up. We keep the silence in which the word can go on being heard.
That is the whole of Xebechani engineering, and most of Xebechani religion, and you may now stop
underlining.

You would have wanted the streets, so here are the streets. Xuthal has no stairs; it is ramps to
the crown, wide spirals cut to the shoulder of a Xudra, because a city is built for its largest
citizen or it is a cage with plumbing. Our doorposts are written in warmth — inscriptions laid in
veins of slow heat, so that at dusk the streets cool into legibility and the city reads itself to
anyone with proper eyes. There are no locks: a people who see heat cannot be crept up on, and
what cannot be crept up on does not invent the lock. When I first walked your Armora I took the
locks for reliquaries, and was not entirely wrong. On the undersides, where the shadow falls on
cloud, hang the rain-gardens that feed us, tended head-down with a patience your farmers would
call piety.

And there you would have met the Xudra, whom your world has never met. The world believes my
people are the Xidhe — the silk-voiced ones we post abroad the way you post sentries. It has met
our ambassadors and believes it has met us. More than half of all Xebechani drawing breath are
Xudra, and no Xudra leaves Xebech: the mass and the patience of the race, huge, heavily scaled,
brief-lived, and — record this — the only caste that laughs. They keep the ramps, the gardens,
and the shells of the Pools, and they sing exactly once a year, at the Rebirth: a counting-chant
with one number in it. The number changes. I will come to that.

### Of doors, which are our statecraft

You will want the state next, and there is no state as your registries mean it: no throne, no
assembly, no tax but duty. Our statecraft is doors.

Eleven centuries before your birth, my predecessors walked the dead network of the Ancients and
taught stretches of it to carry again — the **True Ways**, your books call them, and for once
your books are exact. We revived those roads so that the peoples of Ymir could be gathered
quickly if the demons came again. It was Xebechani feet on them that summoned the First Council
of Peoples; your Church's chronicles write "the peoples were summoned," in the passive voice, and
we have never corrected them — correction is a tax the corrected must pay. And when you, in your
red book, argued that a door which cannot be moved can be watched, and that warders should stand
at every gate — that was a Xebechani argument, my pupil. We had been making it for two thousand
years to anyone who would hold still. You were the first to make it in a language princes fund.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *"Statecraft," it says. Hear it plainly: the Xebechani are the **portal-caste** — grown in the
> Pools by the empire whose network the True Ways merely re-lit, gate-keepers by design as a
> pointer is a pointer by design. They did not adopt a policy of doors; they* are *the policy,
> bred and issued. That is why the dead network answered their hands and no one else's, and why
> the priest, cataloguing two millennia of gate-craft, never once names the doors' maker. It
> knows the name. It is keeping the house.*

### The second question

Your second question you asked with both hands flat on my table, which among your people is
rudeness and among mine is prayer: *what is behind the doors of Xanadu?*

Walk it with me; you have the time. Xuthal flies; **Xanadu refuses to**. Three days up from the
river-cities, through jungle that thins politely as if excused, and then the fortress — not
built, you would say at first sight, but *decided*: grey-green stone with the grain of an
argument, and no gate where a siege would want one. Inside, the corridors are all of one length;
measure any and you have measured them all, and the measuring has driven three of your profession
quietly home. At the centre is the room the rolls call the Quiet Room. In it stand the four
**Runes of Power**, the great engines of the Ancients, keyed — you recorded the correspondence
with a scholar's shiver, and the shiver was the correct instrument — one to each realm of your
art. Around them, six sockets, empty, where the six Relics sat as seals for three and a half
thousand years. We keep the sockets dusted. Do not mistake it for hope. It is bookkeeping.

You saw the beginning of the age those sockets opened. So I will tell you the part no chronicle
has thought to ask after. Two warm strangers came to Xanadu in the year the world turned,
carrying the gathered six. The chronicles keep them nameless, and a chronicle's agreement should
be kept by somebody. But ask your own question of the event, meterer: how did two warm bodies
cross a fortress garrisoned by a people who see heat, unremarked, to the one room that matters?
They did not. **The doors were opened from within.** We judged the hour dark enough that the
weapons of the Ancients should wake, and we let the key turn. Whether we judged rightly, the age
you did not live to see is still deciding.

And your Curator once promised his readers — I have read every leaf of his book; we read
everything, eventually — that where the four Runes now stand, and in whose keeping, was matter
for the historical chapters. His historical chapters never paid. I will. **They stand where they
have always stood.** Unsealed is not the same as spent. In whose keeping? In ours — in the sense
that a lamp keeps a flame. One change only, since you knew the place: Xanadu was the coldest
house in Xebech, the one building our children could not read. Since the Tide it is warm. Every
eye in the river-cities can see it glowing, three days away, like a word beginning.

> see the navigator's diary in Chapter V, ninth of Spider; he swore he would be called a liar, and
> this chapter is his acquittal. — *the Curator*

### The page you turned

Now a question you never asked, because you were courteous exactly once. You asked about our
north exactly once, and when I did not answer, you turned the page. Here is the page.

In the year 2020 of your reckoning the dead came up out of our north — not our dead only, but the
dead of every field and wreck that coast had ever taken, and behind them a will we never named.
Attend to that, you who filed the gods under poetry: we know the six names of the powers beyond
the sky, and we never learned the name of the thing that took our north. It showed no face and
sent no terms, and things that want nothing cannot be treated with. We fought it for seventy-five
years — which is how long it takes a patient people to accept a fact — and in 2095 we drew the
border ourselves and stepped behind it.

Understand what the war cost, because the chronicles say our numbers were nearly halved and let
the sentence rest as if it were about soldiers. Our dead are not buried, pupil. They are
*continued*: the body goes to the Pools, and the Pools give back a child. Every corpse that thing
took and raised was a cradle robbed — for three generations we watched our unborn walk toward us
with weapons. That is the arithmetic under the chronicle, and it is why we speak of the war only
to the dead. We renamed the land as we left it: **Loquitchatzotech**, which your maps render
*Isle of Death*, which is close, and wrong. The word means, more nearly, *the place where our
dead are not ours*. Your sailors note that no birds fly there. The birds are wiser than the
sailors.

### Of the Vigils, which you feared

Of our worship you never asked at all. You were frightened of it, which was the single most
intelligent thing about you, and I honoured the fear too much to relieve it.

The Cult of the Stars does not keep your calendar of months and feast-days; we keep **Vigils** —
six watches in each Cycle, one for each of the Elder Ones, timed by the risings of their stars. A
Vigil is not a festival. Nothing is eaten, nothing is sung; the Xion take the sky's account, the
reckoners enter it, and the entry is the rite. We are not asking our gods for anything. We are
confirming their position, as your harbour-masters confirm a ship still at anchor. For two
hundred years after your death the Vigil of the Observer required no priests at all: the feast
hung in the sky where every warm nation could see it and call it an omen. You saw the red moon
rise. You did not see it furl, around 3260, withdrawing as an eye is withdrawn from a lens when
the observation is complete. We entered that in the rolls too. The entry reads: *seen enough*.

And there is one of the Six to whom we no longer pray. To that one we perform only the
**Apology**: once a Cycle, in silence, every caste, the whole night. You will not learn from me
what we apologise for. You will not learn it from anyone living; that is what *eldest* is for.

> *In Chapter I, I called their waiting the patience of a jailer's servant. Here is that patience
> at home. The Vigils are not adoration; they are **shift-work** — six watches kept over six
> sealed prisoners, logged nightly for thirty-five centuries. The Apology is addressed to the one
> their forebears reached for and botched: the Xebechani strand of the single Catastrophe, told
> from the temple side. And mark the composure about the failing Pools that follows. A servant
> does not mend the household past the master's homecoming — and by their own sky-ledger, the
> homecoming has begun.*

### The third question

Your third question was in your last letter, which I did not answer. You offered to come to the
Pools with your meters; you had numbered the wagons; you had already chosen the road. I read the
letter three times and repaired the seal, and you died — briefly, the way your people do
everything — before I found the sentence I wanted. This letter is that sentence, grown long.

Here is what you would have seen. The body is carried down at dusk, by Xudra, singing their one
song: the counting-chant, the tally of all our people, revised at every carrying. The Pools take
the body as still water takes a stone — without ceremony, which is the ceremony. A season later,
if all is well, the water gives back a child, and the tally rises by the number it fell. It has
been *all is well* for longer than your civilisation has existed.

It is no longer all is well. Some children come back wrong now — unfinished, or folded strangely,
or perfect and empty. We do not put them away, as your villages do your Marked; we name them, and
they hold the softest offices. And some do not come back at all. The chant's number has fallen,
Cycle upon Cycle, for two hundred years. You would write: *the series converges*. We are dying of
a lost instruction, in good order, without complaint — the complaint would change nothing, and it
would be overheard.

Twice, since you died, they have carried *me* down at the proper age and in the proper manner,
and twice the water has declined me. The attendants dried me and said nothing; there is no rite
for it — I checked; I keep the rolls. I am the eldest of the reckoners because the machine that
continues us has twice refused to conclude my case. Do not condole. It is instructive, being
one's own discrepancy.

And once, in the Chaos after your death, when the dead walked even your tidy streets, we opened
the deepest door in Xanadu — the one that opens *away* — and sent a part of every caste through
to the far sanctuary, until the sky's eye furled. Most came home. Some keep the far side still. I
will not describe it: you would have wanted to meter it, and there are doors even a dead pupil
must be refused. It is the last refusal, little archmage. I am sorry for all of them.

For five hundred years I have wondered whether your meters would have read what our rites cannot.
The wondering is the nearest thing to doubt my kind is issued. So take the sentence I could not
write while you lived: I do not know that you could have saved the Pools. **I know that I did not
let you try.** Between those two sentences lies the whole distance between my people and yours,
and I am no longer certain we stand on the better side of it.

> ⟡ **IN THE MARGIN (GM)** ⟡
> *The "far sanctuary" is on no map of Ymir because it is not on Ymir. It is **X-01** — the
> artificial planet the empire built to regulate its Portal network, still turning in the dark —
> and Xanadu's deepest door opens onto it. The Chaos-era evacuation was partial and mostly
> temporary: the people returned, Xuthal still flies, the Vigils are kept. But some stayed; and
> the priest's refusal to describe the far side hides the one fact that would remake this
> chapter's grief. Out there, children are grown in Pools that do not fail, because the
> switchboard was never out of maintenance. Somewhere past the sky stands a Xebech that is not
> dying. Ask, at your table, which is the backup of which — and who decided.*

### Last

I have read your book, as I said. You ended it with a question you called poetry, having spent
two hundred pages calling mine poetry: should the Essence prove a breath after all, you wished
your kind to learn, before it exhales, *who is breathing*. I could not have taught you that. But
I may correct the question — you always permitted the correction, in front of those candles of
yours that hated me. Do not ask who breathes. Ask what a held breath is *for*. One holds a breath
to dive. One holds it to hide. One holds it to listen. The world will learn which, in a while
that will feel long to it, and is not.

Three questions; three answers; and the things the answers lean on. If the warm peoples read this
over your shoulder — they will; they are incapable of not — let them note what the only full
account of my country cost: one brief warm woman who asked *on* and not *how*, and waited, with
instruments, and died waiting. That is the epitaph I would have cut for you, had anyone asked the
stone-cutters of Xuthal: SHE ASKED ON.

Sleep on, warm pupil. You were very brief, and the night is long, and I find I mind its length
differently now.

Written in Xuthal, in the Cycle your reckoners number 3518, on the ninth night of Tortoise, when
nothing watched back.

> decide for your own table whether the pupil reads nothing. The Dreamlands are not fussy about
> what the dying dreamed last.*

> *Curator's note.* I have had a fair copy sent to the College of the Seventh Circle at Armora,
> where Sir Drusilde's reading-room is kept as she left it. The librarian, a practical woman in

> late*, and laid it on the desk; I am told the room is visited rather more than it was. The

> — *the Curator*
