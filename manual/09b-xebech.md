# IX·B · A LETTER TO A DEAD PUPIL
## *being Xebech, the Land, in the only account of it this book will ever hold*

**Set down by Xochiyayotl, Xattva Loremaster of Xuthal, Star-Priest of the Cult of the Stars, eldest of the reckoners of Xebech; addressed to Sir Drusilde of Armora, Archmage and Directress of the College of the Seventh Circle, five centuries dead; deposited unasked at the Crystal Seat.**

> *Curator's note.* I must account for this chapter, because I cannot account for it. Twice I
> wrote to Xuthal asking for the land's own leaf — the one great blank in this book — and twice
> my letter came back unopened, its seal not broken but *repaired*, which I am told is Xebechani
> for no. Then, this spring, a Xidhe courier laid the following packet on the steps of the Crystal
> Seat, waited for no receipt, and was gone; and the packet bears an address no courier alive can
> serve. Sir Drusilde of Armora died five hundred years ago. Her treatise stands third in this
> volume; her teacher's cosmogony stands first; and between those two chapters, it appears, a
> correspondence has continued that only one party could still write. I sat with the impropriety
> a long while. To print a private letter is theft; to print a letter that cannot be delivered
> may be the only delivery left. What decided me was arithmetic, which she would have approved:
> this is the only account of Xebech that exists, or ever will. The eldest people has refused
> every census, every embassy of scholars, every chair of every college — and then answered, once,
> a dead woman's questions. I do not believe a being that old misdirects its mail. I believe the
> address is exact, and that the rest of us are permitted to stand behind her and read.
> — *the Curator*

To Sir Drusilde of Armora, of the Seventh Circle, wherever the warm dead are kept.

You will forgive me a delay before a single answer. Answers mislead; delays do not. You have been
dead for most of five hundred years, and I am told by the living that this makes my letter
improper. The living are brief, and briefness makes them anxious about sequence. I taught you
better. A letter arrives when it arrives; the only impropriety is inaccuracy, and I have waited
precisely as long as the answers required.

The compilers at the Crystal Seat asked me for an account of my country. I do not make accounts
for the living; the living interrupt. You never interrupted. You did something far worse, which I
find I have missed for five centuries: you waited, visibly, with instruments.

**You asked me once what Xuthal stands on. You were the only one who asked *on*, and not *how*.**
I told you the question was not well-formed. That was untrue, and I knew it was untrue when I
said it, and I have kept the knowledge the way your College keeps its dram of Arvo — in a lined
case, taken out on anniversaries. It was the best-formed question I had been asked in a long age.
But the answer was ours, and you were not ours. Now you are dead, which is a citizenship of its
own, and older than mine. Attend, then. Three times you asked and three times I refused, and a
teacher who outlives a pupil inherits the pupil's questions with the rest of the estate. I intend
to settle it in full.

### The first question

Xuthal stands on permission.

The *how* I give you in one sentence, for your ghost's method: the city rests where a working of
the Ancients holds it, as a note is held by a singer who has not yet chosen to breathe in. The
*on* is this: it stands on a word spoken so long ago that no one remembers the shape of the
mouth. We do not keep the city up. We keep the silence in which the word can go on being heard.
That is the whole of Xebechani engineering, and most of Xebechani religion, and you may now stop
underlining.

You would have wanted the streets, so here are the streets. Xuthal has no stairs; it is ramps to
the crown, wide spirals cut to the shoulder of a Xudra, because a city is built for its largest
citizen or it is a cage with plumbing. Our doorposts are written in warmth — inscriptions laid in
veins of slow heat, so that at dusk the streets cool into legibility and the city reads itself to
anyone with proper eyes; your kind sees only blank basalt, and finds us doorless and dour. There
are no locks. A people who see heat cannot be crept up on, and what cannot be crept up on does
not invent the lock; when I first walked your Armora I thought the locks were reliquaries, and I
was not entirely wrong. On the undersides, where the shadow falls on cloud, hang the rain-gardens
that feed us, tended head-down with a patience your farmers would call piety.

And there you would have met the Xudra, whom your world has never met. The world believes my
people are the Xidhe — the silk-voiced ones we post abroad the way you post sentries, whose gaze
your sailors describe with such nervous poetry. The world has met our ambassadors and believes it
has met us. More than half of all Xebechani drawing breath are Xudra, and no Xudra leaves Xebech;
they are the mass and the patience of the race, huge, heavily scaled, brief-lived, and — record
this, it will be believed from you where it would not be from me — they are the only caste that
laughs. They keep the ramps, the gardens, and the shells of the Pools, and they sing exactly
once a year, at the Rebirth, a counting-chant with one number in it. The number changes. I will
come to that.

### Of doors, which are our statecraft

You will want the state next, and there is no state as your registries mean it: no throne, no
assembly, no tax but duty. Our statecraft is doors.

Eleven centuries before your birth, my predecessors walked the dead network of the Ancients and
taught stretches of it to carry again — the **True Ways**, your books call them, and for once
your books are exact. We did not build them; we revived them, gate by gate, and we did it so that
the peoples of Ymir could be gathered quickly if the demons came again. It was Xebechani feet on
those roads that summoned the First Council of Peoples; your Church's chronicles write "the
peoples were summoned," in the passive voice, and we have never corrected them — correction is a
tax the corrected must pay, and we are not collectors. When your own age broke open and you, in
your red book, argued that a door which cannot be moved can be watched, and that warders should
stand at every gate — that was a Xebechani argument, my pupil. We had been making it for two
thousand years to anyone who would hold still. You were the first to make it in a language
princes fund.

### The second question

Your second question you asked with both hands flat on my table, which among your people is
rudeness and among mine is prayer: *what is behind the doors of Xanadu?*

Walk it with me now; you have the time. Xuthal flies; **Xanadu refuses to**. Three days up from
the river-cities, through jungle that thins politely as if excused, and then the fortress — not
built, you would say at first sight, but *decided*: a mass of grey-green stone with the grain of
an argument, no two gates alike and no gate where a siege would want one. Inside, the corridors
are all of one length. Measure any of them and you have measured them all, and the measuring
changes nothing and has driven three of your profession quietly home. At the centre is the room
we do not name aloud; the rolls call it the Quiet Room. In it stand the four **Runes of Power**,
the great engines of the Ancients, each keyed — you recorded the correspondence with a scholar's
shiver, and the shiver was the correct instrument — one to each realm of your art. Around them,
six sockets, empty, where the six Relics sat as seals for three and a half thousand years. We
keep the sockets dusted. Do not mistake it for hope. It is bookkeeping.

You saw the beginning of the age those sockets opened; you wrote two years downstream of it. So I
will tell you the part no chronicle has thought to ask after. Two warm strangers came to Xanadu
in the year the world turned, carrying the gathered six. The chronicles keep them nameless, and a
chronicle's agreement should be kept by somebody, so I keep it. But ask your own question of the
event, meterer: how did two warm bodies cross a fortress garrisoned by a people who see heat,
unremarked, to the one room that matters? They did not. **The doors were opened from within.** We
judged the hour dark enough that the weapons of the Ancients should wake, and we let the key
turn. Whether we judged rightly, the age you did not live to see may still be deciding.

And your Curator once promised his readers — I have read every leaf of his book; we read
everything, eventually, it is one of the consolations of the span — that where the four Runes now
stand, and in whose keeping, was matter for the historical chapters. His historical chapters
never paid. I will. **They stand where they have always stood.** Unsealed is not the same as
spent. In whose keeping? In ours — in the sense that a lamp keeps a flame. And one change only,
since you knew the place: Xanadu was the coldest house in Xebech, the one building our children
could not read. Since the Tide it is warm. Every eye in the river-cities can see it glowing,
three days away, like a word beginning.

### The page you turned

Now a question you never asked, because you were courteous exactly once. You asked about our
north exactly once, and when I did not answer, you turned the page. Here is the page.

In the year 2020 of your reckoning the dead came up out of our north. Not our dead only — the
dead of every field and every wreck that coast had ever taken, walking, and behind them a will we
never named. Attend to that, you who filed the gods under poetry: we know the six names of the
powers beyond the sky, and we never learned the name of the thing that took our north. It showed
no face, sent no terms, wanted nothing we could withhold. Things that want nothing cannot be
treated with. We fought it for seventy-five years, which your chronicles record as if it were a
long time; it was seventy-five years because that is how long it takes a patient people to accept
a fact. In 2095 we drew the border ourselves and stepped behind it.

Understand what the war cost, because the chronicles say our numbers were nearly halved and let
the sentence rest as if it were about soldiers. Our dead are not buried, pupil. They are
*continued*: the body goes to the Pools, and the Pools give back a child. Every Xebechani corpse
that thing took and raised was a cradle robbed — a birth conscripted into the enemy's line. For
three generations we watched our unborn walk toward us with weapons. That is the arithmetic under
the chronicle, and it is why we do not speak of the war, and why I am speaking of it only to the
dead. We renamed the land as we left it: **Loquitchatzotech**, which your maps render *Isle of
Death*, which is close, and wrong. The word means, more nearly, *the place where our dead are not
ours*. Your sailors note that no birds fly there. The birds are wiser than the sailors.

### Of the Vigils, which you feared

Of our worship you never asked at all. You were frightened of it, which was the single most
intelligent thing about you, and I honoured the fear too much to relieve it.

The Cult of the Stars does not keep your calendar of months and feast-days; we keep **Vigils** —
six watches in each Cycle, one for each of the Elder Ones, timed not by the sun but by the risings
of their stars. A Vigil is not a festival. Nothing is eaten, nothing is sung; the Xion take the
sky's account and the reckoners enter it, and the entry is the rite. We are not asking our gods
for anything. We are confirming their position, as your harbour-masters confirm a ship still at
anchor. For two hundred years — after your death — the Vigil of the Observer required no priests
at all: the feast hung in the sky where every warm nation could see it and call it an omen. You
saw the red moon rise. You did not see it furl, around 3260, withdrawing as an eye is withdrawn
from a lens when the observation is complete. We entered that in the rolls, too. The entry reads:
*seen enough*.

And there is one of the Six to whom we no longer pray. To that one we perform only the
**Apology**: once a Cycle, in silence, every caste, the whole night. You will not learn from me
what we apologise for. You will not learn it from anyone living; that is what *eldest* is for.

### The third question

Your third question was in your last letter, which I did not answer. You offered to come to the
Pools with your meters. You had numbered the wagons; you had already chosen the road. I read the
letter three times and repaired the seal, and you died — briefly, the way your people do
everything — before I found the sentence I wanted. This letter is that sentence, grown long.

Here is what you would have seen. The body is carried down at dusk, by Xudra, who sing their one
song: a counting-chant, the tally of all our people, revised at every carrying. The Pools take
the body as still water takes a stone — without ceremony, which is the ceremony. A season later,
if all is well, the water gives back a child, and the tally rises by the number it fell. That is
the Rebirth. It has been all is well for longer than your civilisation has existed.

It is no longer all is well. Some children come back wrong now — unfinished, or folded strangely,
or perfect and empty. We do not put them away, as your villages do your Marked; we name them, and
they hold the softest offices, and they are loved, I believe, more than the whole. And some do
not come back at all. The chant's number has fallen, Cycle upon Cycle, for two hundred years.
You would write: *the series converges*. We are dying of a lost instruction, in good order,
without complaint — the complaint would change nothing, and it would be overheard.

Twice, since you died, they have carried *me* down at the proper age and in the proper manner,
and twice the water has declined me. The attendants dried me and said nothing; there is no rite
for it — I checked; I keep the rolls. So I am the eldest of the reckoners because the machine
that continues us has twice refused to conclude my case, and I no longer ask why. Do not condole.
It is instructive, being one's own discrepancy.

And once, in the Chaos after your death, when the dead walked even your tidy streets, we opened
the deepest door in Xanadu — the one that opens *away* — and sent a part of every caste through
to the far sanctuary, until the sky's eye furled. Most came home. Some keep the far side still.
I will not describe it. You would have wanted to meter it, and there are doors even a dead pupil
must be refused. It is the last refusal, little archmage. I am sorry for all of them.

For five hundred years I have wondered whether your meters would have read what our rites cannot.
The wondering is the nearest thing to doubt my kind is issued. So take the sentence I could not
write while you lived: I do not know that you could have saved the Pools. **I know that I did not
let you try.** Between those two sentences lies the whole distance between my people and yours,
and I am no longer certain we stand on the better side of it.

> switchboard was never out of maintenance. Somewhere past the sky stands a Xebech that is not
> dying. Ask, at your table, which settlement is the backup of which — and who decided.*

### Last

I have read your book, as I said. You ended it with a question you called poetry, having spent
two hundred pages calling my poetry poetry: should the Essence prove a breath after all, you
wished your kind to learn, before it exhales, *who is breathing*. I could not have taught you
that. But I may correct the question — you always permitted me the correction, in front of those
candles of yours that hated me — so attend, one last time. Do not ask who breathes. Ask what a
held breath is *for*. One holds a breath to dive. One holds it to hide. One holds it to listen.
The world will learn which, in a while that will feel long to it, and is not.

Three questions; three answers; and the things the answers lean on. If the warm peoples read this
over your shoulder — they will; they are incapable of not — let them note what it took to obtain
the only full account of my country ever given: one brief warm woman who asked *on* and not
*how*, and waited, with instruments, and died waiting. That is the epitaph I would have cut for
you, had anyone thought to ask the stone-cutters of Xuthal: SHE ASKED ON.

Sleep on, warm pupil. You were very brief, and the night is long, and I find I mind its length
differently now.

Written in Xuthal, in the Cycle your reckoners number 3518, on the ninth night of Tortoise, when
nothing watched back.
