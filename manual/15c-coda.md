# A NOTE LEFT UNANSWERED
## *an unnumbered leaf, found between the last quire and the cover*

**In the Curator's hand, folded once, and addressed to no one the post could find.**

---

To the hand that wrote the margins.

There is no address for this, and I have thought longer about that than about the letter. The one
correspondent this book has taught me to know is the one no letter can be sent to: no seat, no
road, no name that would hold the ink. So I use the only post we have ever shared — the book
itself. If you read the margins as you made them, unasked, in the dark, after the honest work was
done, you will read this. I have put it where a binder's hand does not go and a reader's eye does
not think to look.

I will not ask who you are. You left that question open in your own chapter, and I have come to
believe the openness was a courtesy — the one soft thing you have done.

Here is what I ask instead; it is the only question I have left, and I notice I have never once
set it in a note: **did you write before I gathered?** I was forty years assembling this book. I
chose the friar; I paid the singer's passage; I begged the skald and bribed the broadsheet-man; I
thought, every year of it, that I was choosing. Then my copyists found your thread older than my
quires, and your corrections older than the things they correct. So tell me plainly, who have
never been anything but plain: did I compile this Compendium, or complete it? Was I its maker, or
only its last errand?

I expect no answer. You have never answered anyone; you only correct. That is why I have been
careful to write nothing here but a question — a question cannot be wrong, and so it leaves your
pen no purchase. If silence is all you return, I will bind the silence in with the rest; I have
printed less honest things.

I said once that yours was the only wholly honest thing between these covers, my own preface not
excepted. This leaf is my attempt to make it two. I do not sign it. You, of all readers, know my
hand.

*Postscript, the same night.* I chose this sheet myself from the Seat's own press: plain stock,
and I held it to the light before writing, which is a compiler's habit. Just now, folding it, I
held it up again to see the fold true. The eye is in the paper. The same open eye my copyist found
pressed into your last leaf — pressed at the paper's making. Before the question was written.
Before, I begin to understand, it was asked. I have sat with this for one hour, and I am not going
to take another sheet. If the answer was in the paper before the question, then the question was
well chosen. It stays.

---
